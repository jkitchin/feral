* 07:15 :step2:ssids-det-floor:research:
Continuing session after user said "get started". First queued
item is the SSIDS scale-invariant 2x2 det floor port. Consulted
spral-expert for exact test_2x2 source at ldlt_tpp.cxx:88-119
and its relationship to the Duff-Reid growth bound.

Key findings:
- SSIDS's ldlt_tpp.cxx:101-106 computes detscale = 1/maxpiv
  and tests |detpiv| < max(small, |detpiv0|/2, |detpiv1|/2)
  against the *scaled* determinant.
- This is scale-invariant by construction: all three quantities
  rescale by |c| when the block is scaled by c.
- The classical cancellation rule: |a - b| < |a|/2 means more
  than one bit of significance is lost. max/2 (not min/2)
  protects against the case where the larger summand cancels
  catastrophically.
- small = 1e-20 (SSIDS datatypes.f90:260), NOT sqrt(eps). Near
  the underflow boundary, dead-zero detection only. Using
  sqrt(eps) was the regression trap in 2026-04-13-05.
- feral's existing Duff-Reid growth bound
  (|a22|*rmax + amax*tmax)*u > |det| is *algebraically
  identical* to SSIDS's u*max(x1,x2) < 1 at lines 115-118 after
  multiplying through by |det|. So feral already has that
  guard; the missing piece was the scaled cancellation floor.

Research note: dev/research/ssids-scale-invariant-det-floor.md.

1. MC64 matching-based scaling (Phase 2.4) — could reduce

   residuals below 1e-8.

   r = b - Ax in quad precision, solve A*dx = r in double,
   update x. Standard technique for cond-limited systems.

Neither is within scope of a single-session triage. Documenting
the finding here and moving on to item (2): +1 ACOPP30 dense/

* 09:00 :acopp30-0004:deep-triage:
Wrote examples/triage_acopp30_0004.rs to determine whether the
(71,137,1) result was caused by (A) Knight-Ruiz equilibration,
(B) zero_tol being too strict, or (C) natural-order pivot
sequence picking a rank-deficient pivot that AMD ordering
avoids.

Experiments:
- factor_single_front (eq + natural)    : (71, 137, 1)
- factor_frontal (no eq + natural)      : (71, 137, 1)
- factorize_multifrontal (AMD)          : (72, 137, 0) MATCH
- factor_frontal (no eq + nat + zt=0)   : (71, 137, 1)

Equilibration is NOT the cause (no-eq gives same result).
zero_tol is NOT the cause (zt=0 gives same result). **Ordering
is the cause**: AMD reorders the pivot sequence so no column
becomes implicitly rank-deficient during elimination.

Walking the dense kernel's D block-by-block confirmed the
structural pattern: positions k=0..56 are all 2x2 blocks of the
form
  [[+0        , h_i],
   [h_i       , -eps]]
each contributing (1,1,0) to inertia. Then at **k=58, 1x1 pivot
d=+0.000e0 exact**. This is the (0,0,1) that pushes inertia
from (72,137,0) to (71,137,1).

Interpretation: by the time the natural-order elimination
reaches k=58, the previous 29 2x2 blocks have implicitly
consumed that column's rank, leaving a literally-zero column.
needs_refinement kicks in and the solution still achieves
1e-14 residual via iterative refinement (which uses A, not D,
for the residual). So the SOLUTION is correct; only the
reported inertia is off by one.

In exact arithmetic, inertia is a matrix invariant (Sylvester).
In finite precision, it depends on how pivoting interacts with
rank-deficiency-in-intermediate-Schur-complements. AMD avoids
the bad intermediate ordering; natural order hits it.

Decision: accept this as an architectural limitation of the
"no symbolic analysis" design of factor_single_front. Fixing
it would require adding AMD preorder, which negates the step 1
wrapper's design (cheap dense-kernel access without symbolic).

Net: factor_single_front improved 67 ACOPP30 matrices vs
baseline factor(), at the cost of 1 ACOPP30 matrix where
natural-order differs from AMD-order. Net +66 improvement.
The remaining ACOPP30_0004 discrepancy is a fundamental limit
of natural-order processing on KKTs where 29 successive zero-
diagonal 2x2 pivots implicitly consume rank; it cannot be
closed at the kernel level, only by preordering. Closing
the last 1-matrix gap requires rerouting the bench's
ACOPP30_0004 case through factorize_multifrontal OR adding
AMD preorder to factor_single_front as a future task.

* 12:00 :phase-2.4.1:plan:
Per the plan ordering, the entry point for closing the tail is
Phase 2.4.1 blocked dense LDLᵀ with block_size = 64. Consulted
faer-expert for the reference design at
faer/src/linalg/cholesky/bunch_kaufman/factor.rs. Key findings:

- lblt_blocked (factor.rs:700) is a right-looking panel with
  deferred trailing update, xLASYF-style.
- W workspace of shape (n-k) × block_size holds unscaled
  columns; D-scaled L columns live in-place in A.
- Peek-ahead update_and_offdiag_argmax (factor.rs:461)
  reconstructs the would-be-updated column into W only when
  needed for pivot selection; the rest of the trailing block
  stays stale until the final syrk.
- Final BLAS-3 call: matmul(Ar, StrictTriangularLower, W,
  Al^H, -1) at factor.rs:684. D is never formed; it is folded
  into the L side of the product.
- Panel boundary: kmax = block_size - 1 at factor.rs:515 so a
  2×2 pivot can extend through the last slot without
  overflowing. Partial 2×2 state is NOT carried across panels.
- faer does not do pivot rejection. feral's
  try_reject_1x1_frontal needs special handling on the blocked
  path — cleanest option: on rejection, panel terminates
  cleanly with n_elim < block_size, caller applies the partial
  trailing update, and falls back to scalar for one step.
- faer defaults: block_size=64, crossover at n≤64.

bench, allocation dominates.

* 15:15 :faer-expert:vectorization:decision:
Consulted faer-expert on whether faer's blocked BK uses
a vectorized kernel. Verdict: yes, and that's where the
entire speedup lives. factor.rs:684 calls
linalg::matmul::triangular::matmul which lowers through
matmul_simd → pulp-dispatched register-blocked micro-kernel
(MR×NR with masked SIMD tails, x86-v3/v4 feature-gated).
The panel body itself (lblt_blocked_step) is plain scalar
Rust — the win is entirely in the trailing-update GEMM.

Implication: copying faer's loop structure without a SIMD
micro-kernel gives zero speedup. Exactly what 2.4.1a's bench
showed. Scalar loop reorder is a no-op on total flops; without
vectorization there's nothing to win.
