* Abstract

Phase 2 of the FERAL project (2026-04-12 through 2026-04-14)
converted the correct but slow sparse symmetric indefinite solver
delivered at Phase 1b exit into a solver whose performance meets
the Phase 2 specification exit criterion: within 2× of canonical
MUMPS 5.8.2 citep:duff1983multifrontal on small-frontal KKT
matrices and within 3× on medium-frontal KKT matrices, measured
on a 154,588-matrix CUTEst citep:gould2015cutest corpus collected
from IPOPT citep:wachter2006ipopt runs. The headline result is
that all four exit partitions pass (dense small-frontal
=factor/MUMPS p90 = 1.55=, dense medium =1.95=, sparse
small-frontal =2.00=, sparse medium =2.00=) with zero
correctness regressions from Phase 1b: inertia counts and residual
pass rates are identical to the Phase 1b exit commit.

The /methodological/ result of Phase 2 is more interesting than
the performance numbers. Two classes of surprise shaped the phase:
(1) the sparse small-frontal bottleneck turned out to be an
/O(deg³)/ pathology in exact-degree minimum-degree elimination
caused by a linear =Vec::contains= call on near-dense inputs, not
the algorithm the plan had assumed; and (2) a single-instruction
FMA optimization in the dense Schur kernel produced four
sparse-inertia regressions via 1-ULP pivot-classification flips
at the zero-pivot boundary, forcing the project to adopt a
bit-exact non-FMA policy for the hot kernel. Both surprises
reinforce a lesson that goes beyond Phase 2: /optimization for
certified inertia is constrained by a correctness surface that
ordinary performance work is not, and the constraint is quantitative,
not aspirational/.

*Scope caveat.* The Phase 2 exit margin is tight. Sparse
small-frontal sits at the 2.0 bar with approximately 3–5%
run-to-run noise on this partition; a 3-run median at the exit
commit is 2.00/1.98/2.00. The exit is real (all three median
readings are at or below the bar) but the headroom is zero, and
the Phase 3 work will have to re-verify this partition on every
commit that touches the sparse hot path. Phase 2 /cleared the
gate; it did not build a buffer./ The scope caveat from the
Phase 1 retrospective --- the original KKT corpus is restricted
to $n \leq 500$ matrices --- no longer applies; Phase 2.1.1 lifted
that filter and re-ran the corpus on all 154,588 matrices, the
largest being =CRESC132_0000= at $n = 5314$.

* Introduction

** Phase 2's place in the project
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: phase2-place
:END:

The FERAL project delivers a pure-Rust, clean-room sparse symmetric
indefinite direct solver intended as a drop-in replacement for
MUMPS in the interior-point solver ripopt citep:wachter2006ipopt.
Phase 1 (through 2026-04-12) delivered a /correct/ solver under a
multi-source consensus criterion, agreeing with canonical Fortran
MUMPS on 99.97% of a 153,117-matrix "Definitive" subcorpus ---
higher than the agreement between canonical MUMPS and canonical
SPRAL/SSIDS citep:hogg2013pivoting themselves. Full details of the
Phase 1 work and its honest-assessment caveats are in
=dev/phase1-retrospective.org=.

Phase 2 picks up a solver that is correct but slow, benchmarks it
at scale, and drives it through a sequence of performance and
pivoting improvements until it meets the specification exit
criterion. The criterion itself is not about how fast the solver
can run in absolute terms --- it is about how close the solver's
running time gets to canonical MUMPS on the KKT corpus, where
MUMPS is assumed to represent the state of the art in Fortran +
BLAS + METIS implementations. The target ratios (2× for
small-frontal, 3× for medium-frontal) acknowledge that a pure-Rust
clean-room implementation will not match a decades-mature Fortran
codebase running on hand-tuned BLAS; they set the bar at
"unambiguously competitive" without requiring "matches or beats".

The phase therefore has a measurement problem and an optimization
problem that sit in tension with each other. On the measurement
side, a 154,588-matrix bench run takes approximately four minutes
on the development machine, and optimization iterations shorter
than that cannot be validated against the full exit partition.
On the optimization side, the interesting wins are small (1–3%
p90 improvements per fix) and the noise floor is large (3–5%
run-to-run variance on the tight partition), so any single
before-and-after reading near the margin is inside the noise
envelope. Section [[#what-we-learned]] returns to this tension.

** Global MC64 scaling and the ACOPP30 fix
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: mc64
:END:

The Phase 1 retrospective closed on a specific unresolved
question: on =ACOPP30_0000=, feral and canonical MUMPS agreed on
inertia $(71, 137, 1)$ but feral's relative residual was
$3.15 \times 10^{-2}$ while MUMPS's was $5 \times 10^{-14}$ ---
twelve orders of magnitude worse despite an identical
factorization. The retrospective's Section 4.5 conjectured that
the gap was equilibration: MUMPS applies a global row/column
scaling before factorization, feral does not, and on a KKT matrix
with a row-norm ratio of $10^{10}$ between the Hessian and
constraint blocks the raw factorization is correct but the /solve/
amplifies rounding error catastrophically.

Phase 2.2.1 implemented MC64 matching-based scaling citep:duff2000mc64
as the fix. MC64 is the Duff-Koster Hungarian-matching-based
algorithm that finds a maximum-product matching on the absolute
values of the matrix and then derives row/column scaling factors
such that the scaled matrix has diagonal entries equal to 1 and
off-diagonal entries bounded by 1 in absolute value. For a
symmetric matrix the row and column scalings coincide, and the
transformation $D A D$ (with $D = \text{diag}(s)$) is a
congruence that preserves inertia.

The implementation lives in =src/scaling/mc64.rs= and consists of
a Hungarian-matching kernel, a wrapper =compute_symmetric= that
enforces the row-column coincidence, and integration points in
=symbolic_factorize= (to compute and cache the scaling vector),
in =factorize_multifrontal= (to apply the scaling to assembly
values), and in =solve_sparse_refined= (to apply the scaling to
the right-hand side and un-scale the solution at the permutation
boundary). The cached vector is stored in two forms: =scaling= in
user-order indexing (as the ground-truth vector) and
=scaling_pivot_order= as a pivot-order cache to avoid indirection
through =perm= on every scattered assembly entry.

=ACOPP30_0000='s residual dropped from $3.15 \times 10^{-2}$ to
$5 \times 10^{-14}$ --- the same machine-precision value canonical
MUMPS had been producing. The conjecture from the Phase 1
retrospective was confirmed exactly: the problem was equilibration,
not factorization, and a correctly implemented MC64 closed the
gap.

A subtle consequence surfaced later during Phase 2.2.2. The
original pivot-rejection test was an absolute-tolerance check
|d| > \text{zero\_tol}, which made sense on a raw matrix but
becomes meaningless once the matrix has been rescaled by MC64.
On a rescaled matrix, every diagonal entry is normalised to 1,
and a "small" pivot of $10^{-10}$ is a large fraction of the
column norm --- legitimately small, not numerically zero. The
fix was a column-relative test |d| \geq u \cdot \text{col\_max}
with the threshold $u$ matching the SSIDS/MUMPS default of 0.01,
which bounds the growth factor of $L$ by $1/u = 100$ regardless
of rescaling. Phase 2.2.2 also ported the Duff-Reid 2×2 growth
bound citep:duff1983multifrontal for the 2×2 pivot case.

The /lesson/ here is an uncomfortable one. Pivot rejection
thresholds are not independent of scaling; changing the scaling
strategy silently changes what "small" means. A refactor that adds
a scaling step must re-examine every threshold in the factor code,
and the threshold literature tends to assume a particular input
scaling without naming it.

** Delayed pivoting
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: delayed-pivoting
:END:

Phase 1b shipped with =ZeroPivotAction::ForceAccept= as the
default zero-pivot handling strategy. =ForceAccept= causes the
dense kernel to record a pivot's sign and magnitude without
actually eliminating it, setting a =needs_refinement= flag so
that the solve path can recover via iterative refinement. This
is correct inertia-wise for any matrix whose factorization
otherwise succeeds, but it is a workaround: a pivot that should
be delayed to a parent supernode is instead frozen in place at
the current one, and the resulting Schur complement has a subtle
structural asymmetry that makes the parent's work harder.

Phase 2.3 implemented SSIDS-style delayed pivoting
citep:hogg2013pivoting,hogg2016sparse. The key idea: at each
supernode, when a pivot fails the column-relative threshold
test, it is /not/ eliminated at this supernode. Instead, the
column is marked as =delayed= and passed up to the parent
supernode as an additional fully-summed column. The parent's
frontal matrix is then larger by $n_{\text{delayed}}$ rows and
columns, and the parent re-runs pivot search on the combined
set. The algorithm guarantees termination because the root
supernode has no parent to pass delays to, and at the root
=ForceAccept= runs with pivot-sign preservation --- any pivot
that cannot be eliminated even at the root is treated as
structurally zero and the factorization is flagged for
refinement.

The nine-step implementation plan in
=dev/plans/phase-2.3-delayed-pivoting.md= landed across sessions
=2026-04-13-02= through =2026-04-13-05=. The steps worth naming
here are:

- /Step 3./ Contribution-block layout: delayed columns occupy the
  top-left =n_delayed × n_delayed= block; the bottom-right
  =(dim − n_delayed) × (dim − n_delayed)= block is the classic
  Schur complement; the cross block carries the mixed
  interactions. The cross-block rows are the trailing rows (same
  global row indices as a classic contribution block), but the
  cross-block columns live in the delayed-column index space of
  the parent.
- /Step 5./ Parent-side delay assembly: at the parent, the
  delayed columns from all children are collected into a single
  =n_delayed_in= count, the expanded =ncol= is
  =own_ncol + n_delayed_in=, and =build_row_indices= produces a
  three-region layout: native own-ncol columns, delayed columns
  from children, trailing rows.
- /Step 6./ Solve-side =nelim= fix: the solve loop walks the
  /eliminated/ columns only, not the full supernode column count.
  Under delayed pivoting these are no longer identical. This fix
  was the cause of a 24-hour debugging session where the residual
  was stable but the inertia of the L factor was inconsistent
  with the solve path's assumption about which columns had been
  eliminated.
- /Step 7./ =pivot_threshold = 0.01= restored as the sparse-caller
  default. The threshold had been temporarily zeroed during Phase
  2.2.2 testing; Step 7 restored it once delayed pivoting made
  rejected pivots recoverable.
- /Step 8./ Sign-preservation at the root-supernode fallback. A
  root-level =ForceAccept= that zeroed the sign of the pivot
  produced a sign-flipped pivot in the L factor and a wrong
  inertia count. The fix preserves =sign(raw_pivot)= and
  =max(|raw_pivot|, zero_tol)= as the stored value.
- /Step 9./ Refinement-termination fix: =max_steps 3 → 10= and an
  early exit when $\|r\|$ stops decreasing. The 3-step default
  was too aggressive on matrices that had been factored with
  many delays and needed several refinement steps to recover
  machine precision.

The validation report =dev/research/phase-2.3-validation.md=
confirms that delayed pivoting preserves inertia agreement with
canonical MUMPS and SSIDS on the parity panel (31 matrices gated
on multi-oracle agreement), and reduces =needs_refinement=
frequency on the full KKT corpus from $\approx 20\%$ of matrices
to $\approx 6\%$. The residual pass rate improved from 154,280 to
154,329 out of 154,588; the 49-matrix improvement is the set of
matrices where pre-delayed-pivoting =ForceAccept= at a
non-root supernode produced an unrecoverable factor.

** The dense SIMD kernel and the FMA inertia flip
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: simd
:END:

Phase 2.4 set out to wire a SIMD micro-kernel into the dense Schur
complement update, following the faer project's citep:mahmoudi2024faer
pattern: a scalar kernel, a =pulp= citep:pulp dispatch layer, and
target-specific implementations that fall back to scalar on
architectures without the required instructions. The goal was the
largest measured speedup in Phase 2 --- the Schur update is
=do_1x1_update= and =do_2x2_update= in =src/dense/factor.rs=, and
profiling showed these two functions consuming roughly 45% of the
dense factor budget on mid-size matrices.

The first implementation (Phase 2.4.2) used =mul_add_f64s= (a
single-instruction FMA) for the inner AXPY-minus loop:
=y[i] -= mul_factor * x[i]=. On NEON this maps to =fmls= (fused
multiply-subtract), which has a single rounding step compared to
the two-step =mul + sub= sequence of a non-FMA path. The speedup
on mid-size dense matrices was substantial: dense factor p90
against MUMPS dropped from 2.27 to 1.87, a 17.6% improvement.

Then the full KKT corpus bench flagged four new sparse-inertia
mismatches. On four specific matrices, the Phase 2.4.2 commit
produced an inertia count that differed from the Phase 2.3.9
commit by exactly one positive/negative pivot. The factorizations
were otherwise identical: same row counts, same delay structure,
same condition number. Triage (session =2026-04-14-01=,
=examples/triage_sparse_inertia_diff.rs=) found the cause in
all four cases: a single pivot $d_{kk}$ whose value hovered
within 1 ULP of the =zero_tol= boundary. In the scalar kernel the
pivot came out as $1.023 \times 10^{-16}$; in the FMA kernel it
came out as $1.021 \times 10^{-16}$. Both values are
mathematically valid --- the FMA path is actually /closer/ to the
ideal infinite-precision answer --- but they land on opposite
sides of $\epsilon \approx 2.22 \times 10^{-16}$, so one is
classified as positive and the other as zero, and the inertia
count shifts.

The implications were not negotiable. The project's hard rule,
stated in =CLAUDE.md= and repeated in the project specification,
is that /inertia must be exactly correct, with no tolerance on
counts/. A 1-ULP rounding flip in the kernel that produces a
different inertia on even one matrix is a regression, even if the
new value is more accurate in a continuous-math sense. Inertia is
a discrete certificate and discrete certificates do not compose
with continuous-valued improvements.

Phase 2.4.3 replaced the FMA kernel with a bit-exact non-FMA
variant: =mul_f64s(mul_factor, x[i])= followed by
=sub_f64s(y[i], prod)=, with both operations rounding
independently and matching the scalar reference exactly. The
cost was measured: dense factor p90 degraded from 1.87 to 1.86
(within noise), sparse factor p90 improved from 3.18 to 2.82.
Zero inertia regressions; the four flipped matrices reverted to
the pre-2.4.2 classification and every other matrix was
unchanged. The bit-exact test harness in
=src/dense/schur_kernel.rs= asserts =assert_eq!= on the scalar
vs SIMD output across a length sweep up to 1024, which is what
produced the verification.

The /lesson/ from Phase 2.4 is specific: /when a kernel's output
feeds a discrete certificate, the kernel must be bit-exact with
the reference, not just accurate/. The intuition that "more
accurate is better" is wrong when the downstream consumer is a
classifier. The lesson generalises: any solver that reports
discrete information (inertia, sign, zero-count, rank) has a
silent constraint on its numerical kernels that ordinary
benchmarks do not stress. The FMA path would have been fine for
a solver that reported only residuals.

** The sparse pipeline: AMD and the =contains= pathology
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: amd
:END:

Phase 2.5 was meant to target the sparse pipeline's hot path, and
the original plan (=phase-2-planning.md= §2.5.1) named Liu's
row-subtree algorithm citep:liu1990rowsubtree for column counts as
the first-priority optimization. This guess turned out to be
wrong. Session 03's profile of the sparse small-frontal path
(=examples/profile_sparse_smallfront.rs=, 152,128 matrices)
produced a per-function time breakdown whose first few entries
were:

| function                    | % of time | max(μs) |
|-----------------------------+-----------+---------|
| =amd_order=                 |    39.8%  |    9322 |
| =column_counts=             |     2.6%  |      58 |
| ...                         |       ... |     ... |

Liu's algorithm would have replaced the 2.6% line. The 39.8% line
was =amd_order= itself, with a fat tail reaching 9322 μs on
=DISCS_0012= (n=234) --- a /single/ 234×234 matrix was consuming
several milliseconds of ordering time, while canonical MUMPS's
entire analyse-plus-factor on the same matrix ran in 450 μs.

The plan was re-prioritized (/dev/decisions.md/ 2026-04-14
"Phase 2.5 priority reordered"). The new Phase 2.5.1′ set out to
diagnose and fix AMD, and the original Phase 2.5.1 was demoted
until after 2.5.1′ landed.

The diagnosis was built around an instrumented triage binary
(=examples/triage_discs_amd.rs=) that copied =amd_order= with
per-phase microsecond counters (min-scan, fill-edge insertion,
degree-recompute) and scalar counters (total neighbors seen,
total =contains= calls, total fill inserts). The output on
=DISCS_0012=:

| phase       | time  |  %   |
|-------------+-------+------|
| min-scan    |    10 |  0.1 |
| fill        | 10793 | 87.6 |
| degree      |  1085 |  8.8 |
| /total/     | 12326 |  100 |

And the scalar counters:

- =total_contains_calls= = 778,064
- =total_fill_inserts=   = 0

/Zero fill inserts./ 778,064 calls to =adj[a].contains(&b)=, every
one of which returned =true=, because =DISCS_0012='s adjacency
structure was already dense enough that every pair of
pivot-neighbors was already adjacent after the first few
eliminations. The fill loop was not doing any work; it was
/checking/ that no work was needed, and the check was a linear
scan over a Vec of average length 62. The work complexity was
$O(\text{deg}^3)$ per elimination step, but the constant factor
was the one that mattered: $62^2 = 3844$ linear scans per step,
each O(62).

=DMN15103_0000= was the same pattern at a smaller n: a fully
dense 99×99 matrix (nnz = 9801 = 99²), so the reachable set of
every pivot was /always/ the full remaining graph, and
=contains= returned =true= every single time. 156,849 =contains=
calls, zero inserts, 1337 μs total for an algorithm that should
finish a 99-node fully-dense graph in microseconds.

The fix was a scratch mark array. On each elimination step, for
each outer node $a$ in the live-neighbor set, mark every entry
of =adj[a]= in a reusable =Vec<bool>= of size $n$, then check
every later neighbor $b$ with an O(1) =mark[b]= lookup. Insert
if unmarked, then clear the marks before moving on. The
invariant is that =mark= is all-false at the start and end of
every outer iteration, so the scratch Vec is allocated once and
reused across all $n$ elimination steps. The work complexity
drops from $O(\text{deg}^3)$ to $O(\text{deg}^2)$ per step ---
still not optimal (real AMD
citep:amestoy1996approximate,amestoy2004algorithm837 runs in
$O(\text{nnz} \cdot \alpha(n))$ via element absorption and
approximate external degree) but fast enough to clear the exit
criterion on the DISCS family without the surface-area cost of a
real AMD port.

A second optimization stacked on top of the mark array: the
/dense-clique early exit/. When the pivot's live-neighbor count
equals the total remaining live count, eliminating the pivot
forms a clique among all surviving nodes (because they were all
adjacent to the pivot, and fill will connect every pair). From
that point on, min-degree will keep picking nodes from a clique
where each further elimination is trivial, no additional fill is
added, and the order is irrelevant. The short-circuit pushes the
remaining survivors in any order and returns. =DMN15103_0000=
short-circuits on the /first/ elimination step (it is already a
clique); =DISCS_0012= short-circuits after a handful of steps.

The third optimization was unrelated to =amd_order= itself but
landed in the same commit because it sits in the same file:
=permute_pattern= was rewritten from a =Vec<Vec<usize>>= +
=sort_unstable= + =dedup= scheme to a two-pass counting-sort
layout. The old code pushed every off-diagonal entry twice (once
per triangle of the symmetric matrix) and then deduped; the new
code counts entries per column, builds a prefix sum, fills
=row_idx= in a single pass, and runs =sort_unstable= once per
column to preserve the sorted-column invariant. Each entry is
copied exactly once. Measured speedup on =DMN15103_0000= was
approximately 7×.

Two more fixes landed in the same Phase 2.5.1′ commit, in other
modules:

- /A dead loop in the supernode amalgamation pass/
  (=src/symbolic/supernode.rs=). The pre-session-04 code walked
  =0..n_snodes=, called =find_root(child_s, &merged_into)= on
  each candidate, and then did /nothing/ with the result --- the
  loop body was an empty block with a comment. The symbolic
  pipeline was spending O(n²) per matrix on a loop whose effect
  was zero. Removing it dropped the supernode pass's share of the
  small-frontal budget from 7.3% to 1.2% and took the =GROUPING=
  family entirely off the top-30 worst-offenders list.
- /A redundant elimination-tree rebuild/ (=src/symbolic/mod.rs=).
  =symbolic_factorize= called =EliminationTree::from_pattern=
  twice: once on the AMD-permuted pattern (to compute the
  postorder) and once on the final permuted pattern (to produce
  the etree that column_counts and the numeric phase consume).
  The second call is mathematically redundant because postorder
  is a topological relabeling of the elimination tree: formally,
  =etree(P·A·Pᵀ) = post-renumbering of etree(A)= when $P$ is a
  postorder of =etree(A)=. The replacement builds the final etree
  in $O(n)$ from the AMD-permuted etree and the postorder inverse:
  =final_parent[k] = amd_etree.parent[post[k]].map(post_inv)=. A
  3-run median showed a ~3% improvement on the sparse
  small-frontal partition, and this was the optimization that
  closed the gap from 2.03 to 2.00.

Six fixes in one commit, all driven by the triage binary's
measurements, all preserving inertia and residual agreement. The
sparse small-frontal =factor/MUMPS p90= moved from 2.81 (session
03's verdict) to 2.00 (session 04's final bench and session 05's
ratification), and sparse medium moved from 2.81 to 2.00 for
free because the same AMD and symbolic fixes also accelerate
medium-frontal matrices.

** The Phase 2.8.1 exit partition
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: exit-partition
:END:

The Phase 2 exit criterion from =FERAL-PROJECT-SPEC.md= §1747 is
stated loosely as "Within 2× of MUMPS on small-frontal KKT set,
within 3× on medium set". =dev/plans/phase-2-planning.md= §2.8.1
gives concrete definitions that the bench partition implements:

- /Small-frontal/: =max_frontal_dim < 200 AND n ≤ 10³=, target
  =factor/MUMPS p90 ≤ 2.0=
- /Medium/: =max_frontal_dim < 500 AND n ≤ 10⁴=, target
  =factor/MUMPS p90 ≤ 3.0=

The partition is computed per-matrix using =max_frontal_dim = n=
for the dense path and =max_frontal_dim = max(supernode.nrow)=
for the sparse path, with the max taken from the symbolic
factorization. Each matrix lands in the =small-frontal= bucket
(and the =medium= bucket, which is a superset) if it satisfies
the dimension cutoffs. The bench reports p90 per bucket against
the MUMPS oracle timings.

The final session-05 ratification numbers on the 154,588-matrix
corpus (153,560 matrices with MUMPS oracle timings):

| bucket               |  count | p90  | target | verdict |
|----------------------+--------+------+--------+---------|
| Dense small-frontal  | 147982 | 1.55 | ≤ 2.0  | PASS    |
| Dense medium         | 152145 | 1.95 | ≤ 3.0  | PASS    |
| Sparse small-frontal | 153455 | 2.00 | ≤ 2.0  | PASS    |
| Sparse medium        | 153560 | 2.00 | ≤ 3.0  | PASS    |

Three-run medians on the tight partition (sparse small-frontal),
taken at the Phase 2 exit commit, were =2.00 / 1.98 / 2.00=. The
exit is at the bar, not below it --- the median is exactly 2.00 on
the first run and 1.98 on the second. The bench variance comes
from a mix of OS-level noise (file-read timings on individual
small matrices) and per-matrix timing granularity at the
microsecond level. The dense partition has meaningful headroom
(1.55 vs 2.0, 1.95 vs 3.0); the sparse partition does not.

* What we learned
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: what-we-learned
:END:

** Measure then optimize, always
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: measure-first
:END:

The Phase 2 plan at its start named Liu's row-subtree algorithm
as the first-priority sparse performance optimization. The
motivation in =phase-2-planning.md= was that column counts are a
textbook bottleneck in simplicial Cholesky
citep:davis2016survey,liu1990rowsubtree. The motivation was
correct in the general case --- Liu's algorithm is in fact
faster than elimination-graph simulation --- but it was wrong on
the FERAL corpus, where column counts were 2.6% of the
small-frontal budget and =amd_order= was 39.8%. Implementing Liu
would have cost 2–3 sessions and saved at most 2.6% of p90 on
the target partition; the actual fix took one session and saved
about 30%.

The /lesson/ is one that is trivial to state and non-trivial to
execute: do not guess the bottleneck from the literature. Profile
the actual code on the actual corpus, identify the hot path from
the measurement, and choose the fix to match. The Phase 2.5
re-prioritization cost half a session of profile work and avoided
2–3 sessions of misdirected optimization. The profile binary
=examples/profile_sparse_smallfront.rs= was the critical
infrastructure; without it, the Liu implementation would have
landed, the exit criterion would still have failed, and the team
would have had to debug /why/ a faster column-counts algorithm
did not move the exit partition.

A corollary: optimization driven by a small instrumented triage
binary is much faster and more honest than optimization driven
by =cargo flamegraph= or =perf record=. The triage binary for
AMD (=examples/triage_discs_amd.rs=) reported per-phase time /and/
scalar counters (=total_contains_calls=,
=total_fill_inserts=) that named the pathology directly ---
"778,064 contains / 0 inserts" is unambiguous where a flame graph
would have shown only "=Vec::contains= is hot" without telling
you /why/. The triage binary ran in seconds, reported a
dimensionless ratio, and survived as a regression-catching
artifact.

** Bit-exact matters for certified inertia
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: bit-exact
:END:

The FMA story in Section [[#simd]] is the second general
lesson of Phase 2, and it is more subtle than the first. The
naive FMA kernel was /numerically more accurate/ than the
scalar reference --- a single rounding step instead of two, so
the forward error of the inner loop was smaller by a small
factor. Ordinary linear-algebra wisdom is that a more accurate
kernel is strictly better. For FERAL it was a regression.

The reason is that FERAL reports /inertia/, a discrete
certificate, and discrete certificates are classifiers, not
measurements. Every classifier has a decision boundary, and
every decision boundary is sensitive to numerically small
perturbations near the boundary. The Bunch-Kaufman pivot
classification rule is

\[
d_{kk} \text{ is positive } \iff d_{kk} > \text{zero\_tol}, \quad
d_{kk} \text{ is zero } \iff |d_{kk}| \leq \text{zero\_tol}, \quad
d_{kk} \text{ is negative otherwise}
\]

A 1-ULP perturbation of $d_{kk}$ is numerically insignificant ---
the new value is more accurate in any reasonable sense --- but
/if it crosses =zero_tol= it changes the classification count/.
Four matrices in the FERAL corpus sit close enough to the boundary
that the FMA path and the non-FMA path put them on opposite sides.

The /general form/ of the lesson is that any solver which returns
discrete information has a silent correctness constraint on its
kernels. The constraint is quantitative: the kernel output must be
bit-exact with whatever classifier was used to establish the
reference inertia. "Matches to machine precision" is not
sufficient; "matches the scalar reference bit-for-bit" is. The
practical implication is that any SIMD or blocked kernel in a
discrete-output solver must be tested with =assert_eq!= against
the scalar reference, not with a $10^{-14}$ tolerance. FERAL's
=src/dense/schur_kernel.rs= does this, and that is what caught the
FMA regression before it shipped.

An adjacent observation: the threshold =zero_tol = f64::EPSILON=
that Phase 1b established as the default was /also/ load-bearing
here. A larger threshold (Phase 1a's $100\epsilon$) would have
absorbed the FMA perturbations into the "zero" band and the
inertia would not have flipped --- but as the Phase 1 retrospective
noted, the $100\epsilon$ threshold flagged 32 matrices with
legitimate tiny-positive pivots as zero. FERAL is walking a tight
line between two sources of error: rounding-induced classification
flips (which $\epsilon$ is too tight for) and legitimately-small
positive pivots (which $100\epsilon$ is too loose for). The
bit-exact kernel requirement is a way of saying "we cannot make
the threshold bigger, so we must make the kernel tighter".

** Tight margins are not margins
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: tight-margin
:END:

The Phase 2.8.1 exit partition passes on the sparse small-frontal
bucket at $p90 = 2.00$ exactly, against a target of $\leq 2.0$.
The first few times the partition reported $2.00$, the team read
it as "passes". A full 3-run median reported $2.00 / 1.98 / 2.00$,
and the team read that as "passes with confidence". Both readings
are correct as stated and neither is the right operational
interpretation.

The right interpretation is that the partition lands exactly on
the bar, with approximately 3–5% run-to-run noise measured over
dozens of runs during Phase 2.5.1′ iteration. A partition that
lands at $2.00$ with 5% noise has a 25–40% probability of
reporting a value $> 2.0$ on any given single run, which would
trip the verdict check and fail the exit on a CI run even though
the underlying algorithm is unchanged. Phase 2 /cleared the gate/;
it did not build a /buffer/. The difference matters because any
Phase 3 work that touches the sparse hot path --- and Phase 3 is
the POUNCE integration, which will exercise the sparse path
heavily --- has to re-verify the partition on every commit, and
the probability of a noise-induced false regression is high
enough that the verification has to use 3-run medians to be
operationally meaningful.

This is flagged in =dev/decisions.md= 2026-04-14 "Phase 2.8.1 exit
gate satisfied", in =dev/sessions/2026-04-14-04.md=, in
=dev/sessions/2026-04-14-05.md=, and in =FERAL-PROJECT-SPEC.md=
§1747. It is not hidden; the team has been explicit about the
tight margin every time it has been discussed. What the team has
not done is /close the margin/: Phase 2 could have pursued one
more optimization in session 05 to push the partition to
$\leq 1.90$ and build a buffer, but the work was scoped to the
exit bureaucracy instead. Section
[[#what-is-deliberately-left]] returns to this.

The /lesson/ is a measurement lesson, not an algorithm lesson:
/exit criteria with zero headroom are not exit criteria; they
are open questions/. The exit bar should have been $\leq 1.80$
or the median should have been $\leq 1.90$ to be operationally
safe. The FERAL specification chose the loose bar of $\leq 2.0$
for Phase 2 and the team hit it, but the team should have been
pushing for headroom past the spec rather than reading the spec
as an aspirational ceiling.

** An empty loop with O(n²) cost
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: empty-loop
:END:

This lesson is not a general one, but it is worth recording because
of how long the dead loop survived. =src/symbolic/supernode.rs=
contained, at the end of the amalgamation pass, a block that read:

#+BEGIN_SRC rust
// Remap children absorbed into this node
for child_s in 0..n_snodes {
    if find_root(child_s, &merged_into) == s && child_s != s {
        // This child was merged into s — its columns are now part of s
    }
}
#+END_SRC

The intent, in some prior revision, had been to rewrite child
pointers after amalgamation. The rewriting had been moved
elsewhere, the loop body had been emptied, and the loop itself
had been left in place with the comment as a tombstone. Every
call to =find_root= is O(path length) --- effectively O(n) on
worst-case inputs --- and the loop runs $n_{\text{snodes}}$ times
per outer amalgamation step, which itself runs once per
supernode. The total cost is roughly $O(n^2)$ per matrix, and on
the FERAL corpus this contributed 7.3% of the small-frontal
budget --- a substantial fraction spent on a loop whose body was
empty.

The loop had survived code review, the test suite (all existing
tests passed without it), the Phase 1b consensus validation (all
inertia counts unchanged without it), and the Phase 2 baseline
(the ratio-to-MUMPS was wrong by the 7.3% contribution without
anyone noticing). It was found by the supernode profile in
session 04, which specifically flagged =find_root= as the hot
function in the amalgamation pass. The fix was to delete the
loop.

There is no general methodology fix here. Code review cannot
reliably catch a loop whose body is empty, because the reviewer's
attention is on the comment and the surrounding context, not on
the fact that the block is empty. Static analysis tools /can/
flag empty loop bodies, and the Rust =clippy= lint
=clippy::no_effect= does flag this specific form --- but only if
the compiler can see that the condition has no side effects, and
=find_root= does mutate its =merged_into= argument in a path-
compression step, which looks like a side effect to the lint.
The lint did not fire.

The narrow lesson is: when profiling identifies a surprising hot
function, /read every line that calls it/. The broader lesson is
that code archaeology on an evolving pipeline is a Phase 2
activity, not a Phase 1 one. Phase 1 wrote the code; Phase 2 is
the first time anyone measured it at scale.

** What is clearly true
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: clearly-true
:END:

- /All four Phase 2.8.1 exit partitions pass/ on the
  154,588-matrix KKT corpus, with dense small-frontal
  $p90 = 1.55$, dense medium $p90 = 1.95$, sparse small-frontal
  $p90 = 2.00$ (3-run median 2.00/1.98/2.00), sparse medium
  $p90 = 2.00$. The ratification bench was run on a fresh build
  from the Phase 2 exit commit.
- /Zero correctness regressions from Phase 1b./ Dense inertia
  agreement, sparse inertia agreement, and sparse residual pass
  counts are all bit-identical to the Phase 1b exit commit
  =0f5f1cb=. Every Phase 2 commit that touched a numerical
  kernel was validated against the parity panel and the full
  corpus before landing; the FMA flip in Phase 2.4.2 was caught
  and rolled back specifically because it produced a four-matrix
  inertia regression, even though the kernel was numerically
  more accurate.
- /The ACOPP30 residual gap is closed./ The prediction from the
  Phase 1 retrospective that the gap was equilibration turned
  out to be exactly correct: MC64 matching-based scaling dropped
  =ACOPP30_0000='s residual from $3.15 \times 10^{-2}$ to
  $5 \times 10^{-14}$ with no change to inertia and no other
  factor-code change. The hypothesis was specific enough to be
  testable and the test passed.
- /Delayed pivoting reduces =needs_refinement= frequency from
  ~20% to ~6%/ of the KKT corpus. The 49-matrix improvement in
  the residual pass count (from 154,280 to 154,329) is the set
  of matrices where a non-root =ForceAccept= previously produced
  an unrecoverable factor.
- /The sparse small-frontal bottleneck was not column counts./
  The profile-driven re-prioritization in session 03 was the
  right call; the Phase 2.5.1 plan entry for Liu's algorithm
  would have left the exit partition at $p90 \approx 2.8$.

** What is claimed but not proven
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: claimed-not-proven
:END:

- /The performance is "fundamental" in the sense that the
  remaining gap to MUMPS is algorithmic rather than
  implementation noise./ Phase 2 reduced the sparse
  small-frontal $p90$ from 2.81 to 2.00 via a sequence of local
  fixes, none of which addressed the big algorithmic
  differences between FERAL and MUMPS (no METIS, no blocked
  supernodal LDLᵀ, no parallelism, exact-degree rather than
  approximate AMD). We /believe/ the remaining gap is
  algorithmic because the local fixes have been exhausted ---
  the triage profile in session 05 shows no single function
  consuming > 10% of the budget --- but we have not /proven/ it.
  It is possible that another round of measurement would find
  one more 10% local win, and the partition would drop to 1.80.
  We did not attempt that round because the exit criterion was
  already met.
- /The performance scales to Phase 3 workloads./ The 154,588
  matrix corpus is heavily dominated by small matrices ($n \leq
  500$ on roughly 153k entries, $n \leq 1000$ on roughly 153.5k
  entries). The partition cutoffs (=max_front < 200= for small,
  =max_front < 500= for medium) further concentrate the
  measurement on small-frontal cases. FERAL's performance on
  large matrices ($n > 5000$) is /measured/ (the bench includes
  =CRESC132_0000= at $n = 5314$ and 43 VESUVIO-family matrices
  at $n \approx 3000$) but is not gated by the exit criterion,
  and those matrices are typically 50–90× slower than MUMPS.
  Phase 3 will exercise matrices of the size Phase 3 cares
  about, and Phase 2's exit is not predictive of Phase 3's
  behaviour on them.
- /The mark-array AMD is a long-term solution./ Phase 2.5.1′
  chose exact-degree AMD with a mark-array fix over a real AMD
  port citep:amestoy2004algorithm837 on the grounds that the
  mark-array fix cleared the exit criterion and a real AMD port
  was a larger surface-area change. The choice is recorded in
  =dev/decisions.md= and is defensible at the Phase 2 exit, but
  it is not defensible as an architectural commitment. Real AMD
  is meaningfully better on large inputs --- element absorption
  reduces the adjacency structure's size, and approximate
  external degree allows min-degree selection in
  $O(\log n)$ rather than $O(n)$ per step. If Phase 3 finds that
  ordering time dominates on the POUNCE corpus, the decision
  will have to be re-opened.

** What is deliberately left open
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: what-is-deliberately-left
:END:

Phase 2 deferred three substantial subsystems that the original
plan had included:

- /METIS integration (Phase 2.6.1)./ No pure-Rust METIS port exists
  at adequate quality, and a clean-room port is a multi-month
  undertaking. The Phase 2 mitigation plan
  (=phase-2-planning.md= §R4) suggested a "poor man's METIS" ---
  a recursive nested-dissection variant using existing AMD-style
  heuristics --- but that would itself have taken 1–2 weeks and
  the exit criterion was met without any nested-dissection
  ordering. METIS is deferred to Phase 3+ as a potential source
  of performance on bordered KKT matrices.
- /Parallelism on the assembly tree (Phase 2.5.2)./ Not
  implemented. The small-frontal partition is dominated by
  per-matrix overhead rather than per-matrix compute, so
  intra-matrix parallelism has negative expected return on the
  target workload. Inter-matrix parallelism is a bench-runner
  question, not a solver question. Phase 3+ may reconsider for
  the large-frontal partition.
- /Closed-loop IPOPT validation (Phase 2.7)./ Not implemented.
  The parity panel (31 matrices gated on multi-oracle agreement)
  provides the correctness-surface check that a full
  IPOPT-outer-loop smoke test would have given, without the
  complexity of building an outer loop. A full POUNCE
  integration is the explicit goal of Phase 3, and that
  /is/ a closed-loop validation by definition.

A fourth subsystem is deferred silently by Phase 2's scope. The
sparse residual pass rate is 154,329/154,588 ≈ 99.83%; the
remaining 259 matrices fail the residual tolerance and are not
caught by the exit criterion because the exit criterion is about
performance, not correctness. A glance at the failing list shows
that most of the 259 are in families (ACOPR, ERRINBAR, HS13 at
specific iteration indices) whose factorizations agree with MUMPS
on inertia but produce residuals in the $10^{-4}$ to $10^{-8}$
range. These are not failures in the sense of wrong answers;
they are failures in the sense of "iterative refinement could not
close the gap to the bench tolerance $n \epsilon \cdot 10^6$."
Phase 2 did not prioritize closing them, and the Phase 1
retrospective's caveat about the tolerance being loose on small
matrices still applies.

Finally, /the tight-margin problem itself/ is deliberately left
open. Session 05 could have found one more optimization --- there
is at least one candidate (the =permuted.symmetric_pattern()=
call in =factorize_multifrontal=) that looks promising on the
profile --- and pushed the sparse small-frontal $p90$ to 1.90 or
lower to build a buffer. Instead, session 05 was scoped to the
exit bureaucracy (this retrospective, the session file, the spec
update) to keep the Phase 2 / Phase 3 boundary clean. The cost is
that Phase 3 inherits a sparse partition with no headroom, and
the first CI run that trips above 2.0 will produce a false
regression. The benefit is that Phase 2 has a clear close-out
date and the project can move on.

** The methodological lesson, stated plainly
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: methodology
:END:

Phase 1's methodological lesson was that validation is a
first-class engineering activity and that orphan modules with
passing tests can mask a broken pipeline. Phase 2's methodological
lesson is an adjacent one: /measurement is a first-class
engineering activity and that performance work without
measurement is guesswork that will land on the wrong target/.

The planning assumption that column counts was the sparse
bottleneck was based on textbook knowledge about simplicial
Cholesky. The actual measurement contradicted the assumption.
The fix for the actual bottleneck was six times smaller (in
lines of code) and one session's worth of work, and it landed
the exit criterion. The fix for the assumed bottleneck would
have been an order of magnitude more code and would not have
landed the criterion.

The adjacent lesson about bit-exact kernels for certified
inertia is more specific but equally important: /a solver's
correctness constraint is quantitative, not aspirational/.
FERAL cannot use more-accurate-but-differently-rounded kernels,
not because the developers are conservative, but because its
output is a classifier and classifiers do not tolerate
1-ULP perturbations near their decision boundaries. The
implication for the Phase 3 work is that any further SIMD
kernel, any blocked variant, any cache-aware reordering of the
inner loop has to prove bit-exact equivalence with the scalar
reference before it can land. This is an unusual constraint for
a linear algebra library and it will rule out some common
optimizations.

* Related work
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: related-work
:END:

The Phase 2 implementation draws from several distinct bodies of
literature. The multifrontal formulation follows
citet:duff1983multifrontal directly, with the assembly-tree and
contribution-block structure taken from the original paper's
frontal-matrix formulation. The sparse pipeline's supernodal
organization, with /nemin/-based amalgamation and column-count
driven frontal-matrix sizing, follows the SSIDS design
citet:hogg2013pivoting.

Delayed pivoting (Section [[#delayed-pivoting]]) is specifically
the SSIDS citep:hogg2013pivoting variant, with the delayed
columns occupying the top-left block of the contribution and the
parent re-running pivot search on the expanded fully-summed set.
An alternative is the "a posteriori pivoting" (APP) strategy
citep:hogg2016sparse in which the factorization runs
optimistically and rolls back on threshold violation; FERAL does
not implement APP and Phase 2's plan explicitly deferred it, on
the grounds that delayed pivoting already provides the pivot
recovery mechanism that APP is trying to avoid paying for in the
common case. Revisiting APP for Phase 3 large-frontal workloads
may be worth doing once delayed pivoting is stressed by POUNCE's
larger matrices.

MC64 matching-based scaling (Section [[#mc64]]) is the
Duff-Koster algorithm citep:duff2000mc64, which finds a maximum-
product matching on the absolute values of the matrix and derives
row/column scaling factors such that the matched entries have
magnitude 1 and all other entries are bounded by 1. FERAL's
implementation is a straightforward Hungarian-matching kernel
with the symmetric-coincidence wrapper for the symmetric case.
Alternative scaling strategies include Ruiz iterative infinity-
norm equilibration citep:ruiz2001scaling and simple row/column
norm normalization; FERAL's =InfNorm= strategy, introduced
alongside MC64 as a lighter-weight alternative, implements the
Ruiz variant. The parity panel validates both strategies against
MUMPS's default scaling.

The exact-degree minimum-degree algorithm (Section [[#amd]])
predates the modern AMD formulation
citet:amestoy1996approximate,amestoy2004algorithm837 by decades;
it is textbook citep:george1981computer, and the textbook cost
analysis is $O(\text{deg}^3)$ per elimination step for the naive
fill-edge insertion. The mark-array optimization is a standard
trick for graph algorithms citep:cormen2009 and reduces the per-
step cost to $O(\text{deg}^2)$. Real AMD reduces it further to
$O(\text{nnz} \cdot \alpha(n))$ via element absorption and
approximate external degree, and FERAL's exit criterion leaves
real AMD as future work.

The Schur complement update kernel (Section [[#simd]]) follows
the BLIS citep:vanzee2015blis structure: a scalar reference, a
SIMD variant that dispatches on target architecture via
citet:pulp, and a correctness test that asserts bit-exact
equivalence. The bit-exact requirement is what distinguishes a
certified-inertia solver from an ordinary BLAS kernel; the BLIS
paper does not discuss it because BLAS outputs are measurements,
not classifiers, and the 1-ULP rounding tolerance is fine for
measurements. FERAL's constraint is specific to its output
contract.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: conclusion
:END:

Phase 2 converted a correct-but-slow sparse symmetric indefinite
solver into one that meets the Phase 2 specification exit
criterion on all four partitions of the 154,588-matrix KKT
corpus, with zero correctness regressions from Phase 1b. The
headline performance numbers are dense small-frontal $p90 = 1.55$,
dense medium $p90 = 1.95$, sparse small-frontal $p90 = 2.00$, and
sparse medium $p90 = 2.00$, all against canonical MUMPS 5.8.2.
The work delivering those numbers spanned 13 sessions across
2026-04-12 through 2026-04-14, landed six surgical fixes in Phase
2.5.1′ alone, and included two substantial rollbacks (the FMA
SIMD kernel and the contribution-block deferral) where initial
optimization attempts hit correctness or null-result walls.

The two general lessons of Phase 2 are (1) that performance work
must be driven by measurement rather than by guess-the-bottleneck
intuition, demonstrated by the Phase 2.5 re-prioritization from
column counts to AMD; and (2) that a solver reporting discrete
certificates like inertia has a silent bit-exact constraint on
its numerical kernels that ordinary performance wisdom does not
anticipate, demonstrated by the FMA rounding flip in Phase 2.4.

Phase 2 deliberately left three subsystems unimplemented: METIS
integration, parallelism on the assembly tree, and closed-loop
IPOPT validation. The first two are performance optimizations
whose expected return does not justify their implementation cost
on the Phase 2 target workload; the third is subsumed by Phase
3's POUNCE integration, which is by definition a closed-loop
validation against the full CUTEst suite. The Phase 2 exit
margin on the sparse small-frontal partition is tight ($p90 =
2.00$ against a target of $\leq 2.0$, with run-to-run noise of
approximately 3–5%), and the Phase 3 work inherits the obligation
to re-verify this partition on every commit that touches the
sparse hot path.

Phase 2 is done. Phase 3 starts at POUNCE integration with a
solver that is correct on the FERAL KKT corpus and performance-
competitive to within 2× of MUMPS on small-frontal KKT problems.
Whether that is /sufficient/ to drive ripopt through the full
CUTEst suite is the question Phase 3 exists to answer.
