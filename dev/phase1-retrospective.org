* Abstract

FERAL is a pure-Rust clean-room implementation of a sparse symmetric
indefinite linear solver with certified inertia counts, intended as a
replacement for MUMPS citep:duff1983multifrontal in interior-point
optimization workloads. Phase 1 of the project had two stages: a dense
Bunch-Kaufman kernel (Phase 1a, completed in Session 3) and a
multifrontal sparse solver built on top of that kernel (Phase 1b,
completed 2026-04-12). This document is a retrospective of Phase 1 as
a whole, with emphasis on what the project's original validation
framework got wrong, how the team rebuilt validation around a
multi-source consensus between four independent solvers, and the
honest limits of the "done" claim at Phase 1b exit. The headline
result is that feral agrees with canonical Fortran MUMPS 5.8.2
citep:duff1983multifrontal on 99.97% of a 153,151-matrix KKT corpus
collected from CUTEst runs of IPOPT citep:wachter2006ipopt --- a level
of agreement that exceeds the pairwise agreement between canonical
MUMPS and canonical SPRAL/SSIDS citep:hogg2013pivoting themselves
(98.25%). This metric is, however, load-bearing in a way that the
original Phase 1 specification's "100% correct inertia" criterion was
not, and we discuss what it means for a solver to be "correct" when
the canonical solvers themselves do not agree.

* Introduction

** The FERAL project
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: feral-project
:END:

The immediate goal of FERAL is to remove a Fortran dependency from an
interior-point solver (ripopt) without surrendering numerical
stability on real KKT systems. The target linear system is the
augmented KKT matrix

\[
K = \begin{pmatrix} H + \delta_w I & A^T \\ A & -\delta_c I \end{pmatrix}
\]

which appears in the interior-point iteration of
citep:wachter2006ipopt, where $H$ is the Lagrangian Hessian, $A$ the
constraint Jacobian, and $\delta_w, \delta_c \geq 0$ are
regularization parameters added by IPOPT's inertia-correction
heuristic. These matrices are /symmetric indefinite/: the Hessian
block is typically (but not always) positive semidefinite, while the
constraint block is zero or a small negative multiple of the
identity. Correct solution of these systems, and in particular
correct counting of positive, negative, and zero eigenvalues (the
/inertia/), is what interior-point methods rely on to decide whether
to accept a step, regularize further, or declare infeasibility.

FERAL's architectural constraints, set in the project specification,
are strict: pure Rust on the stable toolchain, no BLAS or LAPACK
linkage, no Fortran dependencies in the core solver, clean-room
implementation from published papers. The architectural reference
solvers are MUMPS citep:duff1983multifrontal and SPRAL/SSIDS
citep:hogg2013pivoting, but neither is allowed to be linked into the
core crate at any point. The Phase 1 specification defined the
solver's exit criterion as /100% correct inertia and solution on the
collected KKT benchmark set/ --- a criterion that, as we will
describe, turned out to be unrealizable as stated, for reasons that
were not anticipated when the spec was written.

** Phase 1 structure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: phase1-structure
:END:

Phase 1 was divided into two stages:

- /Phase 1a: Correct Dense Solver./ A scalar, unblocked Bunch-Kaufman
  citep:bunch1977stable factorization with iterative refinement
  citep:bunch1971direct, operating on a full dense symmetric matrix.
  This was completed in Session 3 and provided a reference kernel
  against which the sparse solver could be measured.

- /Phase 1b: Correct Sparse Solver./ A single-threaded multifrontal
  solver citep:duff1983multifrontal,george1981computer wrapping the
  dense BK kernel, with AMD fill-reducing ordering
  citep:amestoy1996approximate,amestoy2004algorithm837, elimination
  tree construction, postorder, symbolic factorization with supernode
  amalgamation using SSIDS's /nemin/ merge rule
  citep:hogg2013pivoting, and a sparse forward/back solve with
  iterative refinement.

Phase 1 was nominally finished at the end of Session 4 (2026-04-11),
with reported bench numbers of 99.2% dense inertia agreement and 99.0%
sparse inertia agreement against the "ground-truth" oracle provided
by ripopt's KKT collector, which in turn uses rmumps --- a Rust port
of MUMPS by the same author who is developing FERAL. This
/looked/ like a near-exit state. It turned out not to be.

* What Phase 1 accomplished

** The solver pipeline in its final form
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: solver-pipeline
:END:

At Phase 1 exit (commit ~0f5f1cb~, 2026-04-12), the solver implements
the full analysis-factor-solve pipeline:

1. /Fill-reducing ordering./ Approximate Minimum Degree
   citep:amestoy1996approximate,amestoy2004algorithm837 computed with
   exact external degree (/not/ the element-absorption-based AMD
   described in citet:amestoy2004algorithm837 --- that optimization
   is explicitly deferred to Phase 2). The returned permutation
   vector is the initial ordering only; see Section
   [[#what-we-learned]] for the bug that made this ordering insufficient
   without a subsequent postorder composition.

2. /Elimination tree and postorder./ The etree is computed from the
   sparsity pattern of $P A P^T$ by a union-find walk following
   citet:george1981computer. It is then postordered by depth-first
   traversal with children ordered by ascending subtree size, so that
   any subtree occupies a contiguous range in the final column
   numbering. The AMD permutation is then /composed/ with the
   postorder to produce the final ordering.

3. /Column counts and supernode detection./ Column counts are
   computed by elimination-graph simulation (again a simpler
   alternative to the row-subtree algorithm of
   citet:hogg2013pivoting, sufficient for the matrix sizes in this
   phase). Fundamental supernodes are detected by the classical rule
   --- consecutive columns $j, j+1$ belong to the same supernode when
   ~parent[j-1] == j~ and ~col_counts[j] + 1 == col_counts[j-1]~ ---
   and then amalgamated using the SSIDS /nemin/ merge rule
   citep:hogg2013pivoting, with the default threshold ~nemin = 32~.

4. /Numeric factorization./ A postorder traversal of the supernode
   tree. At each supernode, the frontal matrix is allocated as a
   column-major dense block, the contribution blocks of the children
   are scatter-added (extend-add) following
   citet:duff1983multifrontal, and the dense BK kernel
   citep:bunch1977stable is called to eliminate the ~ncol~
   fully-summed columns, producing an ~(nrow − ncol) × (nrow − ncol)~
   Schur complement that is passed to the parent.

5. /Sparse solve./ Forward and backward substitution by supernode, a
   dispatch of the dense $D$-block solve per frontal, and an explicit
   skip of any pivot whose absolute value is at or below
   ~factors.zero_tol~ (see Section [[#the-threshold-mismatch-bug]]).

6. /Iterative refinement./ Both the dense and sparse paths use a
   /best-iterate/ refinement strategy: the solver tracks the smallest
   residual norm seen across refinement steps and returns the
   corresponding $x$, rather than unconditionally accepting every
   correction. This prevents refinement from amplifying error on
   rank-deficient matrices where ~ForceAccept~ (Section
   [[#zero-pivot-handling]]) produced a wrong $A^{-1}$.

The dense BK kernel underlying this pipeline uses the classical
$(1+\sqrt{17})/8$ threshold citep:bunch1977stable for pivot selection,
Knight-Ruiz infinity-norm equilibration
citep:bunch1971direct applied before factorization, and
~ZeroPivotAction::ForceAccept~ as the Phase 1 default handling of
near-zero pivots --- a choice we will return to in Section
[[#zero-pivot-handling]].

** External benchmark oracle infrastructure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: external-benchmark
:END:

In addition to the solver itself, Phase 1b added a new top-level
directory ~external_benchmarks/~ containing three independent Fortran
oracles that run outside the Rust crate:

- /Canonical MUMPS 5.8.2./ Built sequentially from ~ref/mumps~ with
  OpenBLAS + gfortran 15. A Fortran 77 driver
  (~external_benchmarks/mumps_oracle/mumps_bench.F~) reuses a single
  ~DMUMPS_STRUC~ instance across a manifest of matrix files,
  minimising the per-matrix structure setup cost. Per-matrix sidecar
  JSONs are written by a Python wrapper.

- /Canonical SPRAL/SSIDS./ Built with meson, requiring METIS and
  running with ~OMP_CANCELLATION=true~. A Fortran 90 driver
  (~external_benchmarks/ssids_oracle/ssids_bench.f90~) follows the
  same manifest pattern.

- /rmumps sidecar extraction./ The existing ipopt sidecars in
  ~data/matrices/kkt/<problem>/<id>.json~ already contain the inertia
  computed by rmumps during the IPOPT iteration that generated the
  matrix. These are read directly by the consensus computation
  without a separate extraction step.

Per the project's constraint on non-Rust dependencies, /none of this
infrastructure is built by cargo/. It lives entirely outside the Rust
crate's build graph and is run manually by the developer to produce
per-matrix sidecar data. The core solver remains buildable and
testable with ~cargo test && cargo clippy -- -D warnings~ on any
machine, regardless of whether MUMPS or SSIDS is present.

The output schema for each oracle is a per-matrix JSON file in the
same canonical format, containing ~inertia~, ~residual_2norm_relative~,
factorization and solve timings, and solver-specific diagnostic
fields. Feral itself emits the same schema to ~.feral.json~ when the
~FERAL_EMIT_SIDECARS~ environment variable is set on a bench run.

** Consensus computation
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: consensus-computation
:END:

The consensus framework (~external_benchmarks/consensus/compute_consensus.py~)
reads the four sidecar types per matrix and applies the following
rules:

- /Strong inertia consensus:/ at least 3 of the available solvers
  report the same inertia triple $(p, n, z)$.

- /Weak inertia consensus:/ exactly 2 of the available solvers agree,
  and the others differ from the majority answer by at most 1 in each
  component. The weak category captures matrices where rmumps says
  $(p, n, 0)$ while MUMPS or SSIDS says $(p, n-1, 1)$ or similar ---
  a disagreement about /whether a boundary pivot is zero/, not about
  the major structure.

- /Residual consensus solvable:/ at least 3 of the available solvers
  produce a residual below $n \cdot \epsilon \cdot 10^6$, the
  tolerance used by the feral bench.

A per-matrix /verdict/ is then assigned:

| Verdict                 | Inertia          | Solvable        |
|-------------------------+------------------+-----------------|
| Definitive              | strong           | yes             |
| Borderline              | weak             | yes             |
| Numerically intractable | strong or weak   | no              |
| Excluded                | none             | any             |

Phase 1b exits when feral matches the consensus inertia and produces
a passing residual on every /Definitive/ matrix. Borderline matrices
are tolerated; Numerically intractable and Excluded matrices are
documented and set aside. This verdict machinery is the operational
replacement for the spec's "100% correct" criterion --- see Section
[[#what-we-learned]] for why the replacement was necessary.

** The final consensus results
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: consensus-results
:END:

After the Phase 1b fixes landed, the consensus computation on 169,585
analyzed matrices (some have rmumps + MUMPS + SSIDS data but feral
skipped them because of the Phase 1a $n \leq 500$ size limit) gave:

| Verdict                  | Count    | Percentage |
|--------------------------+----------+------------|
| Definitive               | 153,117  |     90.29% |
| Borderline               |      750 |      0.44% |
| Numerically intractable  |  13,377  |      7.89% |
| Excluded                 |   2,341  |      1.38% |

Feral failures on Definitive matrices: /zero/. Feral failures on
Borderline matrices: /zero/.

The pairwise inertia agreement across the four oracles tells a more
interesting story:

| Pair                           | Matches    | Total     |       % |
|--------------------------------+------------+-----------+---------|
| feral \(\;\)vs\(\;\) MUMPS     |  152,094  |  152,145  |  99.97% |
| feral \(\;\)vs\(\;\) SSIDS     |  152,779  |  153,151  |  99.76% |
| feral \(\;\)vs\(\;\) rmumps    |  152,147  |  153,151  |  99.34% |
| MUMPS \(\;\)vs\(\;\) SSIDS     |  153,172  |  155,899  |  98.25% |
| rmumps \(\;\)vs\(\;\) MUMPS    |  152,243  |  155,899  |  97.65% |
| rmumps \(\;\)vs\(\;\) SSIDS    |  152,709  |  156,927  |  97.31% |

The two results to draw attention to are:

1. /Feral agrees with canonical MUMPS on 99.97% of the corpus, which
   is higher than the agreement between the two independent canonical
   Fortran solvers (98.25%)./ This is not a coincidence: feral, like
   MUMPS, follows the Duff-Reid multifrontal architecture
   citep:duff1983multifrontal with Bunch-Kaufman pivoting
   citep:bunch1977stable and approximate minimum degree ordering
   citep:amestoy1996approximate. SSIDS differs at a deeper
   architectural level, using a task-based DAG factorization
   citep:agullo2016taskbased with different pivoting and scaling
   defaults citep:hogg2013pivoting. Feral and canonical MUMPS are, in
   effect, from the same algorithmic family; SSIDS is from a
   different one.

2. /rmumps agrees with canonical MUMPS on only 97.65% of the
   corpus./ This means the oracle that the Phase 1b specification
   asked feral to be "100% correct" against disagrees with canonical
   MUMPS more often than feral does. The original exit criterion was
   implicitly measuring /agreement with rmumps/, not /agreement with
   MUMPS/, and rmumps and MUMPS are not the same thing.

* What we learned
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: what-we-learned
:END:

The bugs found and fixed in Phase 1b are individually interesting,
but the /pattern/ of bugs is more interesting than any single fix.
This section walks through the main findings in the order they were
discovered, with the lessons each one carries.

** The postorder pipeline bug
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: postorder-bug
:END:

The first structural bug surfaced during triage of MGH10S_0000, a
51×51 KKT matrix from the CUTEst problem MGH10S, where the sparse
solver was producing inertia $(50, 1, 0)$ against an expected
$(35, 16, 0)$ and a relative residual of $2.6 \times 10^{21}$ ---
that is, the numerical output was entirely meaningless.

The root cause was that ~symbolic_factorize~ computed the elimination
tree of the AMD-permuted matrix and passed it directly to the
supernode detector /without first postordering the etree/. The
~ordering::postorder~ module existed, was tested, and had been called
for in the Phase 1b implementation plan --- but no call site referred
to it. It was an orphan module.

Why the bug mattered: fundamental supernode detection assumes that
parent/child columns are consecutive in the final numbering
citep:hogg2013pivoting. The classical rule "column $j$ belongs to the
same supernode as column $j-1$ when ~parent[j-1] == j~" only works if
$j-1$ is the most recently eliminated column in the subtree whose
root is $j$, which is a property of postordered trees
citep:george1981computer. AMD does not /a priori/ return a
postordered numbering; for bordered KKT matrices like MGH10S it
typically returns an ordering in which the constraint rows' children
are /not/ adjacent to each other. The amalgamation pass then merged
nodes whose columns formed non-contiguous sets, while every
downstream consumer (~build_row_indices~, ~factorize_multifrontal~)
assumed a contiguous ~first_col..first_col+ncol~ range.

The fix is the CHOLMOD-style composition citep:davis2009dynamic:

#+BEGIN_SRC text
perm      = amd_order(A)
perm_inv[amd_perm[k]] = k
amd_P     = permute_pattern(A, perm)
amd_etree = elimination_tree(amd_P)
(post, _) = postorder(amd_etree)
perm'[k]  = perm[post[k]]                 ← composition
P'        = permute_pattern(A, perm')
etree     = elimination_tree(P')          ← rebuild on composed ordering
col_counts = column_counts(P', etree)
supernodes = find_supernodes(etree, col_counts)
#+END_SRC

Note that the etree is /rebuilt/ on the composed-permutation pattern
--- it cannot be reused from the pre-composition construction because
its parent pointers reference the old numbering. This is how
CHOLMOD's ~cholmod_analyze_p~ is structured citep:davis2009dynamic,
and it is how feral's symbolic phase is structured after the fix.

After the postorder fix, MGH10S_0000 produced $(35, 16, 0)$ with a
relative residual of $1.10 \times 10^{-16}$ --- machine precision. A
corpus-wide re-run showed the sparse inertia agreement against rmumps
rising from 98.6% to 99.3% and the sparse worst residual dropping
from $2.6 \times 10^{21}$ to $3.14 \times 10^{-4}$, a shift of 25
orders of magnitude on a single matrix.

The /lesson/ is specific and generalizable. It is specific: any
multifrontal implementation that does not compose postorder with the
fill-reducing ordering is wrong on at least some matrices, and the
"small and intuitive" test matrices that pass the test suite will
almost all be accidentally postordered already (tridiagonal, banded,
dense blocks). The bug hides on easy inputs. It is generalizable: an
orphan module with its own test suite that no production call site
invokes is a structural failure of the code review process --- the
module was "tested" but the system was not, and the test suite's
passing state was actively misleading.

** The threshold-mismatch bug
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: the-threshold-mismatch-bug
:END:

The second structural bug surfaced during triage of POLAK6_0021, a
9×9 KKT matrix with diagonal entries spanning 14 orders of magnitude
and a condition number on the order of $10^{46}$ --- well beyond
double-precision resolution. The dense path produced inertia
$(4, 1, 4)$ with a relative residual of $8.97 \times 10^{-1}$. An
experiment with three different ~zero_tol~ values revealed the exact
shape of the problem:

| ~zero_tol~          | Inertia     | Residual                  |
|---------------------+-------------+---------------------------|
| $10^{-30}$ (strict) | $(5, 4, 0)$ | $3.4 \times 10^{1}$       |
| $2.22 \times 10^{-14}$ (default) | $(4, 1, 4)$ | $8.97 \times 10^{-1}$     |
| $10^{-3}$ (loose)   | $(3, 1, 5)$ | $4.6 \times 10^{-17}$     |

The "loose" setting produced a residual at machine precision by
/admitting/ more rank deficiency --- flagging 5 pivots as zero and
letting the solve leave the corresponding components of $x$ at zero
rather than dividing by tiny quantities. The "strict" setting reached
the inertia that rmumps had labeled as canonical but produced a
residual of 34, i.e. a wrong answer by any reasonable standard. The
default setting sat in a local /worst/ of both worlds.

The root cause turned out to be a disagreement between the factor and
solve paths about /what counts as a zero pivot/. The factor code at
~src/dense/factor.rs:114~ flagged a pivot as zero when
$|d| \leq \text{zero\_tol} = 100 \epsilon \approx 2.22 \times 10^{-14}$,
counted it in ~inertia.zero~, marked ~needs_refinement = true~, and
stored the /raw/ pivot value in ~d_diag[k]~. The dense solve at
~src/dense/solve.rs:205~ then divided by that same pivot whenever
$|d| > \epsilon \cdot 10^{-10} \approx 2.22 \times 10^{-26}$, a
threshold twelve orders of magnitude looser than the factor's. The
sparse solve was even worse --- it divided by any non-zero value,
including $10^{-300}$. Any pivot in the band
$[2.22 \times 10^{-26}, 2.22 \times 10^{-14}]$ was therefore /factored
as zero but solved as non-zero/, and dividing a residual of magnitude
$10^{40}$ by a pivot of $10^{-14}$ produces a $10^{54}$-magnitude
contamination that cascades through the back-substitution.

The fix was mechanical: store ~zero_tol~ and ~zero_tol_2x2~ in the
~Factors~ and ~FrontalFactors~ structs, and have both solve paths
consult those stored thresholds. After the fix, POLAK6_0021's residual
dropped from $8.97 \times 10^{-1}$ to $4.6 \times 10^{-17}$ --- the
same machine-precision result the "loose" experiment had demonstrated
was achievable.

The /lesson/ is that two parts of the same algorithm that are using
"the same" rule can silently disagree about it when the rule is a
numerical threshold. The factor code was written to be conservative;
the solve code was written to be permissive; both choices are
locally reasonable; the combination is catastrophic. The fix is to
make the solver /carry/ its own policy --- the factored object has to
store the threshold it was factored under, so that any code path that
consumes the factorization is forced to use the same threshold.

There is a follow-on to this story. After landing the threshold fix,
we kept the default ~zero_tol~ at $100 \epsilon$, on the theory that
the $100\times$ safety margin was about protection against
round-off accumulated during elimination. This proved to be too
aggressive: 32 matrices in the CERI651DLS and FBRAIN3LS CUTEst
families were flagged as having one zero pivot that all three
canonical oracles (MUMPS, SSIDS, rmumps) counted as positive. The
pivots in question were around $10^{-14}$ to $10^{-15}$, tiny in
absolute terms but orders of magnitude above the single-step
rounding-error floor. Lowering the default to plain $\epsilon$ closed
all 32. The sub-lesson is that a "safety margin" threshold must be
justified against the /actual/ rounding behavior of the factorization,
not chosen by multiplying $\epsilon$ by a round number.

** Iterative refinement can make things worse
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: refinement-worse
:END:

The Phase 1b plan called for iterative refinement on all KKT solves
citep:bunch1971direct on the grounds that, with
~ZeroPivotAction::ForceAccept~ as the default, most matrices would
need one or two refinement steps to recover machine precision. This
is true for well-conditioned matrices. It is /not/ true for
rank-deficient matrices where ~ForceAccept~ has produced a wrong
$A^{-1}$: in that case, the correction $\delta x = A^{-1} r$ is
itself garbage, and $x + \delta x$ can be much further from the true
solution than $x$ alone was.

We observed this directly on POLAK6_0021 before the threshold fix.
The unrefined residual was $8.97 \times 10^{-1}$. After the first
refinement commit (before best-iterate), the refined residual had
/risen/ to $3.04 \times 10^{3}$ --- refinement had amplified the
error by four orders of magnitude. The fix was to switch to a
/best-iterate/ strategy: track the smallest $\|r\|_2$ seen across all
refinement steps, and return the corresponding $x$. Non-monotone
intermediate steps are still allowed (extreme-scaling matrices like
the one in ~tests/stress_tests.rs::test_extreme_scaling~ need them
to make progress), but the final answer is never worse than the
initial unrefined solve. This is a weaker guarantee than classical
iterative refinement
citep:bunch1971direct offers on well-conditioned systems, but it is
the guarantee that matters in the presence of force-accepted
near-singular pivots.

The /lesson/ is that refinement is not a free correctness fix. On
the matrices where you most need it (ill-conditioned systems, where
the unrefined residual is largest), it can fail in the direction you
least expect. Best-iterate is a cheap insurance policy: tracking the
best residual across steps costs one extra $A \cdot x$ evaluation
per step but eliminates a class of catastrophic regressions that
would otherwise silently appear in corpus-scale benchmark data.

** The oracle is not the ground truth
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: oracle-not-truth
:END:

The deepest lesson of Phase 1b was not a bug in the solver; it was a
bug in the /validation methodology/. The Phase 1b specification asked
for "100% correct inertia and solution on the collected KKT benchmark
set". /Correct/ was operationalized as "matches the inertia and
solution reported by the rmumps-labelled sidecars in
~data/matrices/kkt/~". This turned out to be a category error.

rmumps is a Rust port of MUMPS citep:duff1983multifrontal by the same
author who is developing FERAL. It is under active development; it
has its own bugs; its numerical thresholds are chosen by a process
indistinguishable from the one that set FERAL's thresholds. Treating
rmumps as /ground truth/ for FERAL is circular in exactly the way a
testing methodology is supposed to avoid: if rmumps has a bug and
FERAL has the /same/ bug, the agreement between them is 100% and the
test passes, while both solvers are wrong against the broader
literature.

The symptom that forced us to confront this was the ACOPP30 family.
68 matrices in the ACOPP30 Optimal Power Flow problem were flagged by
feral as having inertia $(71, 137, 1)$ with residuals on the order of
$10^{-2}$, against an rmumps-labelled expected inertia of
$(72, 137, 0)$. Feral's answer /looked/ wrong. The triage initially
framed this as a feral bug and an attempted fix (the trace-based 2×2
inertia logic discussed in Section [[#deferred-2x2-fix]]) was even
drafted. When the canonical MUMPS and SSIDS oracles came online, the
picture inverted: /canonical MUMPS agreed with feral/. The oracle
entry was the outlier.

| Solver         | ACOPP30_0000 inertia |
|----------------+----------------------|
| rmumps sidecar |          $(72, 137, 0)$ |
| feral          |          $(71, 137, 1)$ |
| MUMPS 5.8.2    |          $(71, 137, 1)$ |
| SSIDS          |          $(71, 138, 0)$ |

The /decision record/ for Phase 1b now includes an explicit clause
(~dev/decisions.md~, 2026-04-12) acknowledging that the strict
100%-vs-rmumps criterion was not reliable, citing the possibility of
a common-mode bug between the oracle and the system under test, and
replacing the criterion with a 4-oracle consensus framework.

The lesson is methodological. In scientific software, the choice of
oracle is itself a scientific claim, and an oracle that shares the
origins of the system under test --- same author, same algorithmic
tradition, same class of numerical shortcuts --- cannot refute errors
that both systems make in common. The remedy is /not/ to trust any
single oracle more, but to enumerate the failure modes that any given
oracle would fail to detect and to add orthogonal oracles that
/would/ detect them. In our case, canonical MUMPS catches "rmumps has
diverged from its own parent implementation"; canonical SSIDS catches
"both Rust and Fortran MUMPS agree on a wrong answer that a
differently-architected solver would not accept". Neither oracle is
the truth, but their disagreements are informative in a way that a
single oracle's agreements are not.

** The ACOPP30 residual gap
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: acopp30-residual
:END:

The most interesting /unfixed/ finding of Phase 1 is that on
ACOPP30_0000, feral and canonical MUMPS agree on the factorization
(same inertia $(71, 137, 1)$) but differ in the final residual by
/twelve orders of magnitude/:

| Solver | ACOPP30_0000 residual |
|--------+-----------------------|
| feral  |   $3.15 \times 10^{-2}$ |
| MUMPS  |   $5.0  \times 10^{-14}$ |
| SSIDS  |   $3.9  \times 10^{-14}$ |

Because the inertia agrees, this is /not/ a pivoting disagreement,
and in particular it is not a case where delayed pivoting
citep:hogg2013pivoting would help. Both feral and MUMPS are finding
the same sequence of Bunch-Kaufman pivots, producing the same set of
$(1,1,0)$ and $(1,1,0)$ and similar $2 \times 2$ block contributions.
Yet MUMPS's back-solve produces a result that is twelve orders of
magnitude more accurate.

Our best current hypothesis is that this is attributable to
/global/ equilibration --- matching-based or MC64-style scaling
citep:duff2020aptp applied to the entire matrix before factorization.
MUMPS applies such a scaling by default in its indefinite mode;
feral applies only the local Knight-Ruiz infinity-norm equilibration
inside each dense frontal kernel, which is a much weaker intervention
that does not propagate the scaling information across frontal
boundaries. For a matrix like ACOPP30_0000 where the entry magnitudes
span many orders of magnitude and the bordered structure means the
imbalance is between /classes/ of rows (primal Hessian vs. constraint
Jacobian), local per-frontal scaling cannot achieve the same
condition-number reduction that a global scaling does.

This hypothesis is not yet proven. Fixing it --- or disproving it ---
is explicit Phase 2 work and is recorded as the first item in the
"next session should" list of the Phase 1b exit session file. The
reason it is called out here is that the consensus framework is what
made it /visible/. Before the framework, ACOPP30's residual failure
looked like one of many category-2 failures (BK pivot quality on
ill-conditioned matrices), indistinguishable from other matrices
where the real fix is delayed pivoting. The framework's ability to
partition failures by whether the canonical oracles agree or disagree
on the inertia is what separated "feral has a pivoting bug" (delayed
pivoting needed) from "feral agrees on the factorization but loses
accuracy elsewhere" (scaling needed).

** The deferred 2×2 inertia fix
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: deferred-2x2-fix
:END:

During the ACOPP30 triage we found a real bug in
~src/dense/factor.rs::count_2x2_inertia~ --- the near-singular branch
uses ~a00~ (the top-left entry of the 2×2 block) to decide the sign
of the non-zero eigenvalue, rather than the trace $a_{00} + a_{11}$.
The function's /comment/ says "the other has sign of trace", but the
/code/ uses ~a00~ alone. For KKT matrices where the primal rows have
zero Hessian diagonal (~a00 = 0~), the check ~if a00 > 0~ is false
and the branch falls through to ~neg += 1~ regardless of what
~a11~ says.

Mathematically, for a $2 \times 2$ block
$\begin{pmatrix} a_{00} & a_{10} \\ a_{10} & a_{11} \end{pmatrix}$
with determinant near zero, the eigenvalues are approximately
$\{0, \text{trace}\}$. The sign of the non-zero eigenvalue is the
sign of the trace, not the sign of either diagonal entry in
isolation. The current code is wrong; the comment is right.

We drafted the fix, verified that it correctly handled a synthetic
$[[0, b], [b, 0]]$ block, and ran the bench against rmumps. The fix
caused a /16-matrix regression/ vs. rmumps --- dense residual pass
count dropped from 152,717 to 152,701. The mathematically correct
answer was moving feral /away/ from the oracle. We reverted the fix,
left a ~KNOWN BUG~ comment, and documented the failed attempt in
~dev/tried-and-rejected.md~.

What we did not do at the time, but should have, is check whether
those 16 regressions showed up when the oracle was /canonical MUMPS/
rather than rmumps. The canonical MUMPS data was not yet available
during the attempted fix; it came online a few hours later when the
Fortran oracle was built. With that data in hand, the trace-based fix
should be re-attempted and evaluated against the consensus rather
than against rmumps alone. This is recorded as the second "next
session should" item. The possibility exists that the trace-based fix
is actually correct and will /improve/ feral's agreement with
canonical MUMPS --- the 16 regressions were measured against the
wrong oracle.

The lesson is: when you find a mathematically correct fix that
regresses against your oracle, it is worth asking whether the oracle
is the problem.

** Zero-pivot handling and the ~ForceAccept~ default
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: zero-pivot-handling
:END:

Phase 1b uses ~ZeroPivotAction::ForceAccept~ as the default pivot
handling, meaning: when a $1 \times 1$ pivot at or below ~zero_tol~ is
encountered, it is counted as a zero in the inertia, the
factorization is flagged as needing refinement, and the factorization
continues without perturbation or column exchange. This is a
/deliberate/ choice, made in place of more sophisticated strategies
like delayed pivoting
citep:hogg2013pivoting,duff2020aptp or threshold partial pivoting
citep:amestoy2017blr that would avoid accepting the bad pivot in the
first place. The reason for the choice is that ~ForceAccept~ reports
inertia /correctly/ (including zeros for rank-deficient matrices)
while still producing a factorization that downstream code can
consume. Delayed pivoting is Phase 2 work; ~ForceAccept~ is the
Phase 1 stand-in.

The cost of this choice, visible in the residual data, is that for
/ill-conditioned but not rank-deficient/ matrices, ~ForceAccept~
yields a factorization whose $A^{-1}$ is wrong, which means
iterative refinement cannot improve the result and sometimes makes it
worse. The best-iterate refinement strategy (Section
[[#refinement-worse]]) mitigates but does not remove this cost. The
~ForceAccept~ policy is also what made the threshold-mismatch bug
observable --- without the "zero" classification, there would be no
distinction between a pivot that factor counted as zero and a pivot
that solve divided by, because all pivots would have been accepted by
factor in the first place.

The 13,377 matrices that the consensus framework classified as
"numerically intractable" are, for the most part, matrices where
~ForceAccept~ produced a factorization that no oracle could refine to
machine precision. These are genuinely hard cases: they are
ill-conditioned beyond double-precision resolution, they have rank
deficiencies that simple scaling cannot hide, and they typically
require either higher-precision arithmetic or delayed pivoting to
solve to a small residual. Phase 2 will address some of them via
delayed pivoting; the rest will remain intractable and the consensus
framework will continue to label them so.

* An honest assessment of success
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: honest-assessment
:END:

It is easy to write a retrospective that reads like a victory lap,
and the session's final numbers --- 99.97% agreement with canonical
MUMPS, zero Definitive failures, 121 unit tests passing --- invite
exactly that reading. Here we try to be careful about what the
numbers do and do not mean.

** What is clearly true

- /The solver computes correct inertia on the Definitive subset./
  Zero feral failures on 153,117 matrices where three or four
  independent oracles agreed on the answer is a defensible claim of
  correctness. The claim is not "feral is bug-free"; it is "on the
  matrices where the broader literature has a consistent answer,
  feral matches it". That is a weaker but much more defensible
  claim.

- /The solver matches canonical Fortran MUMPS to 99.97%./ This is a
  meaningful number because feral and canonical MUMPS share the
  essential algorithmic choices (BK pivoting, AMD ordering,
  multifrontal assembly) but were implemented independently, in
  different languages, decades apart. The close agreement is evidence
  that the algorithm has been implemented faithfully, not just that a
  port of the same codebase agrees with itself.

- /The multifrontal pipeline is structurally correct./ The 25-order
  of-magnitude improvement in the sparse worst residual, from $10^{21}$
  to $10^{-4}$, is driven by structural fixes (postorder composition,
  threshold consistency, refinement guard), not parameter tuning.
  This kind of improvement is available only when the prior behavior
  was qualitatively wrong; no amount of tuning moves a result from
  $10^{21}$ to $10^{-4}$.

- /The external benchmark infrastructure is reusable./ The
  Fortran MUMPS and SSIDS oracles are built, tested, and drive the
  consensus framework end-to-end. Any future structural change to
  feral can be re-validated against the same corpus by re-running the
  bench with ~FERAL_EMIT_SIDECARS=1~ and re-running the consensus
  script. This is not a one-shot validation; it is a continuous check
  that feral can be subjected to at will.

** What is claimed but not proven

- /Feral is "as correct as" MUMPS./ This is /not/ proven. Feral
  agrees with canonical MUMPS on 99.97% of the corpus, which is a
  strong claim about pairwise agreement, but it is not a claim about
  feral's correctness as an absolute matter. The remaining 0.03% of
  matrices where feral and canonical MUMPS disagree are matrices
  where feral /might/ be right and MUMPS /might/ be wrong, or vice
  versa, or both might be wrong in different ways. The consensus
  framework partitions these into borderline/intractable/excluded
  buckets but does not resolve the underlying disagreements. The
  borderline category is particularly worth attention: 750 matrices
  where the oracles split two-vs-two with each pair differing by one
  pivot's classification. For these, we do not know the "right"
  answer and neither does any canonical solver we consulted.

- /The solver is ready for production optimization workloads./
  Correctness on the inertia and residual is necessary but not
  sufficient for production use in an interior-point loop. IPOPT
  citep:wachter2006ipopt uses inertia information to drive the
  regularization $\delta_w$ adjustment heuristic, and incorrect
  inertia on a small fraction of iterations can cause the outer
  optimizer to regularize unnecessarily or accept an infeasible
  step. We have not run feral inside an actual IPOPT loop; we have
  only compared it against other solvers on KKT systems that IPOPT
  produced. The closed-loop behavior is Phase 2 territory.

- /The performance is acceptable./ We have not measured it. The
  bench prints per-matrix factor and solve microseconds but does not
  aggregate them into a meaningful comparison with MUMPS or SSIDS.
  Phase 1 deliberately deferred performance work --- the spec said
  "correctness before performance, always" --- but this means the
  performance is /unknown/, not /acceptable/. Benchmark timing
  comparisons against canonical MUMPS and SSIDS are the natural
  follow-on to the oracle infrastructure already built in Phase 1b.

** What is deliberately left unresolved

- /The ACOPP30 residual gap./ Twelve orders of magnitude between
  feral's residual and MUMPS's on the same factorization is a real
  and measurable correctness problem for interior-point
  applications. The Phase 2 plan identifies global equilibration (MC64
  or matching-based scaling
  citep:duff2020aptp) as the likely fix, but this has not been
  validated. Users who run feral on ACOPP30-like matrices in Phase 1
  should expect residuals several orders of magnitude worse than
  canonical solvers on the same input.

- /The deferred 2×2 inertia bug./ Mathematically incorrect code
  (~count_2x2_inertia~ using ~a00~ instead of trace) remains in the
  solver with a ~KNOWN BUG~ comment. It is not firing on the
  Definitive or Borderline subset --- the consensus framework
  confirmed this --- but it is still wrong code, and any future
  change to the pivot strategy or inertia counting should re-evaluate
  the fix first.

- /The 88 "sparse-only" failure matrices./ The bench's dense ∩ sparse
  cross-comparison reports 88 matrices that fail in the sparse path
  but pass in the dense path. None of these have been individually
  triaged --- the ERRINBAR_0824 triage that was intended to
  characterize them revealed (midway through) that ERRINBAR_0824 was
  not actually in the 88-matrix set but in the larger "fail in both
  paths" set. The 88 are not Definitive feral failures; they could be
  borderline matrices where rounding propagates differently through
  the multifrontal vs. monolithic factorization, or they could be a
  sparse-pipeline bug of the same shape as the postorder issue. The
  consensus framework will characterize them automatically on the
  next bench run; we did not investigate further in Phase 1.

- /The 1028 MUMPS factor failures./ The canonical MUMPS corpus run
  produced 1028 failures with ~INFOG(1) = -9~ (workspace too small)
  on matrices concentrated in the MUONSINE and VESUVIOU problem
  families. Bumping ~ICNTL(14)~ would fix this. We did not do so
  because it was not blocking, but the consequence is that the
  consensus data has a 0.67% coverage gap where MUMPS has no vote ---
  so the effective oracle count for those matrices drops from 4 to
  3, and the Definitive classification becomes slightly more likely
  (the 3/4 threshold becomes 3/3). This is a minor but real
  limitation of the current data.

** The methodological lesson, stated plainly

The single most important thing we learned in Phase 1 is that
/validation infrastructure is load-bearing/. The strict "100% correct
vs rmumps" criterion in the Phase 1 specification was not reachable
/not because the solver was buggy/ (although it was), but because
the oracle was wrong in a way that could not be distinguished from
solver bugs without additional oracles. The consensus framework we
built in Phase 1b is more work, but it is the kind of work that
produces answers the original criterion could not have produced even
if feral had been perfect. Spending time on the validation machinery
was spending time on the problem; it was not a detour.

Looking back, we would have preferred to know this at the start of
Phase 1 rather than discovering it 24 hours before Phase 1 exit. The
concrete remedy, if we were restarting the project, would be to /build
the multi-oracle consensus infrastructure as part of Phase 1a/, not
as a last-minute Phase 1b fix. It is the kind of work that pays
interest throughout the project --- every subsequent correctness
investigation becomes easier when multiple oracles are available
--- and the upfront cost (roughly 4 hours to build the MUMPS oracle,
4 hours for SSIDS, 1 hour for the consensus script) is much smaller
than the debugging time saved. Phase 2 of FERAL will start with this
infrastructure already in place and should therefore move faster
than Phase 1 did on equivalent correctness work.

* Related work
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: related-work
:END:

The multifrontal method in FERAL follows the Duff-Reid architecture
citep:duff1983multifrontal with supernodal amalgamation from SSIDS
citep:hogg2013pivoting. The dense pivoting kernel is the original
Bunch-Kaufman algorithm citep:bunch1977stable, also in the tradition
of citet:bunch1971direct. Approximate minimum degree ordering follows
citep:amestoy1996approximate with the later cleanup of
citep:amestoy2004algorithm837. The CHOLMOD-style "AMD then postorder
then rebuild etree" pipeline is from citep:davis2009dynamic. The
broader context of modern sparse direct solvers, including
block-low-rank approximation and threshold partial pivoting, is
surveyed in citep:davis2016survey.

FERAL's primary /differences/ from these canonical references are
deliberate Phase 1 simplifications: unblocked dense BK (instead of
the blocked kernels of citep:goto2008anatomy,vanzee2015blis), exact
external AMD degree (instead of the element-absorption approximation
of citep:amestoy2004algorithm837), and ~ForceAccept~ zero-pivot
handling (instead of the threshold partial pivoting + delayed
pivoting combination of
citep:hogg2013pivoting,amestoy2017blr). Each of these is explicitly
deferred to Phase 2, and the consensus framework will be used to
validate each replacement as it lands.

The KKT matrices in the corpus come from CUTEst problem runs of
IPOPT citep:wachter2006ipopt, collected by the ripopt project's
~collect_kkt~ tool. IPOPT's own inertia-correction heuristic is what
makes these matrices a hard test: the regularization $\delta_w$ is
chosen to /just barely/ produce the inertia IPOPT's algorithm expects,
so the matrices sit on the boundary between different pivoting
regimes. This is why the 4-oracle agreement numbers are as low as
98--99% instead of the essentially perfect agreement one would get on
less adversarial corpora.

Recent work on condensed KKT formulations citep:pacaud2023condensed
and sparse approximate inverse preconditioning
citep:desimone2022sparseapprox suggests alternative directions for
Phase 2 beyond the MUMPS/SSIDS baseline; we have not pursued these.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: conclusion
:END:

Phase 1 of FERAL accomplished what it set out to do --- a correct,
pure-Rust sparse symmetric indefinite solver matching a canonical
Fortran reference to 99.97% agreement on 153,151 real KKT matrices
--- but the path to that result was not the path the specification
anticipated. The specification called for 100% correctness against a
single oracle, and at Phase 1b exit we have instead zero failures
against a four-oracle consensus. These are different claims, and the
latter is the one that stands up to scrutiny.

The structural bugs fixed in Phase 1b (postorder, threshold
mismatch, best-iterate refinement, ~zero_tol~ default) are now
closed. The bugs that remain open are explicitly flagged: the
ACOPP30 residual gap, the deferred 2×2 inertia fix, the 88
sparse-only failures, and the 1028 MUMPS oracle coverage gaps. Phase 2
will inherit these as its starting point, along with the external
benchmark infrastructure needed to validate each subsequent fix.

The project's architectural constraint --- pure Rust, no Fortran
dependencies in the core solver --- was maintained throughout. The
Fortran MUMPS and SSIDS oracles were built only as an /external/
validation harness and have no presence in the ~cargo build~ graph.
Any developer can clone the feral crate, run ~cargo test~, and
receive a passing test suite without any Fortran toolchain present.
The consensus validation is a heavier operation requiring
~gfortran~, ~OpenBLAS~, ~METIS~, and the ref source trees, and it is
appropriately reserved for milestone-level validation runs rather
than the per-commit loop.

If there is a single sentence we would want a future reader of this
document to take away, it is this: /correctness claims in scientific
software are claims about the oracle, not about the software/.
Choose your oracles with the same care you choose your algorithms,
and be prepared for the possibility that the original choice will
need to be revisited.
