* Abstract

We present FERAL (Factored Error-Resistant Algebra Library), an open-source,
permissively licensed sparse symmetric indefinite direct solver implemented
entirely in safe Rust on the stable toolchain. FERAL targets the inner linear
solve of interior-point method (IPM) nonlinear-programming codes
cite:wachter2006ipopt, where the dominant cost is a sequence of factorizations
of structurally identical Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) matrices that are sign-
indefinite by construction and require certified inertia. Existing
production solvers in this niche --- HSL MA57 cite:duff2004ma57, MUMPS
cite:duff1983multifrontal, and PARDISO --- are either non-permissively
licensed, written in legacy Fortran with mandatory MPI infrastructure, or
vendor-locked. The recently developed SPRAL/SSIDS solver
cite:hogg2013pivoting,hogg2016sparse is BSD-licensed but depends on a tuned
vendor BLAS at runtime. None of these solvers can be embedded in a
single-binary, dependency-free Rust application, the deployment target of
modern open-source NLP solvers built on the Rust ecosystem.

FERAL closes this gap. The solver implements a clean-room multifrontal
LDL^{T} factorization cite:duff1983multifrontal with full Bunch-Kaufman
partial-pivot selection cite:bunch1977stable, an SSIDS-style delayed-
pivot mechanism with a rook-rescue fallback cite:ashcraft1998accurate,
and the canonical analysis pipeline of approximate minimum degree
cite:amestoy1996approximate,amestoy2004algorithm837 ordering, elimination
trees cite:george1981computer, and supernodal amalgamation. Numerical
robustness on indefinite KKT systems is delivered through MC64 maximum-
product matching cite:duff2001mc64,duff1999permuting combined with Knight-
Ruiz-style symmetric equilibration cite:knight2014symmetry,curtis1972automatic
and an LDL^{T}-aware ordering preprocessor cite:duff2005symmetric. Two
amalgamation strategies (adjacency and SSIDS-style column renumbering) are
dispatched automatically by a cheap O(n) elimination-tree shape predicate.

We validate FERAL against a multi-source consensus oracle constructed from
canonical Fortran MUMPS 5.8.2, BSD SPRAL/SSIDS 2024 cite:hogg2016sparse, and
a self-consistent feral path. The validation corpus comprises approximately
153,000 KKT matrices generated from CUTEst optimization problems and
production chemical-engineering nonlinear programs. On the corpus tail
FERAL is at parity with or faster than SSIDS while emitting a permissively
licensed binary that has zero non-Rust runtime dependencies. We discuss
the remaining performance gap on a small class of tiny IPM-KKT matrices
where MUMPS's ruthlessly optimized driver still leads, and lay out why
this gap closes in workload-realistic measurements that account for IPM
iteration amortization.

** The role of sparse symmetric indefinite solvers in optimization
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:role
:END:

Modern primal-dual interior-point methods for constrained nonlinear
optimization cite:wachter2006ipopt require, at every outer iteration, the
solution of a saddle-point system

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kkt}
\begin{bmatrix}
W + \Sigma & A^{T} \\
A          & 0
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} \Delta x \\ \Delta \lambda \end{bmatrix}
= -\begin{bmatrix} \nabla \phi_\mu \\ c \end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}

\noindent where $W$ is the Hessian of the Lagrangian, $\Sigma$ is a
diagonal barrier-perturbation term, and $A$ is the Jacobian of equality
constraints. The matrix on the left is symmetric, sparse, and
\emph{sign-indefinite} by construction --- the trailing zero block forces
negative eigenvalues equal to the row count of $A$. The IPM step is
mathematically valid only if the factorization reports an inertia of
exactly $(n_x, n_c, 0)$; mismatched inertia signals the algorithm to
perturb the system and refactorize cite:wachter2006ipopt. Inertia is
therefore not a diagnostic --- it is a correctness invariant of the
solver.

The factorization is also the dominant cost. A typical IPM run performs
$30$--$100$ outer iterations, each requiring one sparse symmetric
indefinite factor of a matrix whose pattern is fixed across iterations
but whose values change. The state of the art is to amortize the
analysis (ordering, elimination tree, supernode partition, symbolic
factorization) once across the entire run, then perform numerical
factorization plus solve at every step.

** The existing landscape and the gap FERAL fills
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:gap
:END:

Production-quality sparse symmetric indefinite direct solvers
fall into four categories:

1. *HSL codes* (MA27, MA57 cite:duff2004ma57, MA86, MA97). Reference-
   quality Fortran implementations developed at Rutherford Appleton
   Laboratory. Restrictively licensed: free for non-commercial academic
   use only.
2. *MUMPS* cite:duff1983multifrontal. Public, BSD-style license. Fortran
   90/MPI codebase. Builds against vendor BLAS, Scotch
   cite:pellegrini1996scotch, ParMETIS cite:karypis1998kway, and
   typically OpenMPI even for sequential use. The dominant production
   solver in IPM codes (used by IPOPT cite:wachter2006ipopt and most
   commercial NLP wrappers).
3. *PARDISO*. Closed-source. Fastest on Intel hardware; vendor-locked.
4. *SPRAL/SSIDS* cite:hogg2013pivoting,hogg2016sparse. BSD-licensed
   modern C++/Fortran implementation. Links a tuned vendor BLAS
   (OpenBLAS, MKL) at runtime. Closest to a "modern open" reference.

None of these solvers ships as a single static binary with zero non-
language runtime dependencies. The gap matters in two ways. First,
deploying an IPM solver in a containerized or embedded setting requires
shipping the entire BLAS/LAPACK stack with the application, which
inflates the binary by an order of magnitude and introduces ABI
fragility across CPU microarchitectures. Second, the recent emergence
of Rust-native scientific computing --- including Faer for dense
linear algebra, ndarray, and Polars --- has created a deployment
ecosystem for which there is no native sparse symmetric indefinite
solver of production quality.

FERAL is a clean-room implementation that fills this gap. The design
constraints are:

- *Pure Rust on the stable toolchain.* No FFI to BLAS, LAPACK, MUMPS,
  METIS, or any other Fortran/C library at runtime. (External Fortran
  oracles in =external_benchmarks/= are test infrastructure, never
  linked into the crate.)
- *MIT license.* Permissive, compatible with both academic research and
  commercial embedding.
- *Certified inertia.* Inertia counts are exactly correct, with no
  tolerance, on every Definitive matrix in the consensus corpus.
- *Multi-source validation.* Correctness is established through a
  three-way consensus across feral, MUMPS, and SSIDS rather than
  against any single reference.
- *Co-designed with the consuming optimizer.* The solver exposes a
  stateful API that mirrors Ipopt's =SymLinearSolver= contract
  cite:wachter2006ipopt, including a two-stage quality-escalation
  protocol (scaling, then pivot threshold) that the outer NLP loop
  drives directly.

This work makes the following contributions:

1. The first production-quality, MIT-licensed, BLAS-free, pure-Rust

4. A multi-oracle consensus framework that classifies validation

** Bunch-Kaufman partial pivoting
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:bk
:END:

The Bunch-Kaufman algorithm cite:bunch1977stable computes a factorization
$P A P^{T} = L D L^{T}$ of a symmetric matrix $A$ where $L$ is unit
lower-triangular and $D$ is block-diagonal with $1 \times 1$ and $2
\times 2$ blocks. The use of $2 \times 2$ blocks is essential: the
1971 result of Bunch and Parlett cite:bunch1971direct established that
$1 \times 1$ blocks alone are insufficient for symmetric indefinite
matrices, since a zero diagonal forces the algorithm to pair two adjacent
columns into a single pivot. The Bunch-Kaufman pivot threshold $\alpha
= (1 + \sqrt{17})/8 \approx 0.6404$ minimizes the worst-case element-
growth bound across the choice between $1 \times 1$ and $2 \times 2$
pivots cite:bunch1977stable.

The /partial/ variant of Bunch-Kaufman, used in essentially all production
codes, restricts pivot search to a single column per step. This bounds
search cost at $O(n)$ per pivot but admits adversarial inputs on which
element growth is unbounded. Practical implementations recover stability
through three layers: a column-relative pivot threshold (typically $u =
0.01$, the MA57 default cite:duff2004ma57), an SSIDS-style delayed-pivot
escape that postpones rejected pivots to the parent supernode
cite:hogg2013pivoting, and a /rook-rescue/ fallback
cite:duffreid1996zeros,ashcraft1998accurate,foster1997rook that performs
an alternating column/row scan to find a stable pivot before declaring a
column delayed.

** Multifrontal LDL^{T}
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:multifrontal
:END:

The multifrontal method of Duff and Reid cite:duff1983multifrontal
organizes a sparse factorization as a postorder traversal of an
elimination tree cite:george1981computer. At each node, a small dense
/frontal matrix/ is assembled from the original matrix entries plus
contributions from already-eliminated children. A partial dense
factorization (here Bunch-Kaufman) eliminates the /fully-summed/
columns of the front; the remaining /Schur complement/ is propagated
upward to the parent as a contribution block. This decomposition
exposes essentially all of the factor work to dense BLAS-like kernels,
which is why multifrontal solvers historically dominate sparse
symmetric direct factorization at scale.

The /symbolic phase/ of the solver computes the elimination tree, column
counts, and supernode partition before any numerical work. Supernodes
group adjacent columns of the elimination tree that share fill structure
and can be eliminated by a single dense kernel call; aggressive
amalgamation (relaxing the strict equality criterion) trades fill for
better dense kernel utilization cite:duff1983multifrontal. The CHOLMOD
codebase cite:davis2009dynamic established the canonical pipeline
(AMD $\rightarrow$ postorder $\rightarrow$ column counts $\rightarrow$
supernode amalgamation) that FERAL adopts.

** Approximate minimum degree
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:amd
:END:

The fill-reducing permutation is the dominant determinant of factor work.
Approximate minimum degree (AMD) cite:amestoy1996approximate,amestoy2004algorithm837
is a quotient-graph-based heuristic that selects pivots by approximate
remaining degree, with element absorption to keep the working graph
compact. AMD is the default ordering in CHOLMOD cite:davis2009dynamic,
MUMPS, and SSIDS, and is FERAL's default. Nested-dissection orderings
(METIS cite:karypis1998fast, KaHIP cite:sanders2011engineering, Scotch
cite:pellegrini1996scotch) sometimes win on grid-structured Hessians but
require external graph-partitioning libraries; FERAL ships pure-Rust
ports of AMD that match the canonical Fortran reference within tolerance.

** Inertia and the IPM correctness invariant
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:inertia
:END:

The /inertia/ of a symmetric matrix is the triple $(n_+, n_-, n_0)$
counting positive, negative, and zero eigenvalues. Sylvester's law of
inertia guarantees that for a congruence factorization $P A P^{T} = L D
L^{T}$, the inertia of $A$ equals the inertia of $D$, which can be read
directly from the $1 \times 1$ and $2 \times 2$ blocks. The IPM
correctness check requires that the KKT matrix have inertia $(n_x, n_c,
0)$, where $n_x$ is the number of primal variables and $n_c$ the number
of equality constraints cite:wachter2006ipopt. A mismatch signals
either rank-deficient constraints, an indefinite Hessian region, or a
numerical pivot failure --- the IPM perturbs the system with a
regularization $\delta_w I$ (primal) and $-\delta_c I$ (dual) and
refactorizes. Inertia therefore must be exactly correct, not
approximately so. FERAL elevates this to a hard constraint:
=Definitive= matrices in the consensus corpus must have inertia agreement
with no tolerance.

** Architectural overview
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:arch
:END:

FERAL is structured as a single multifrontal engine with pluggable
strategies, not as a collection of separate solvers. The high-level
data flow is:

#+begin_src text
CSC matrix
  └── Symbolic phase
        ├── AMD ordering
        ├── Elimination tree (Liu's algorithm)
        ├── Postorder
        ├── Column counts (Liu)
        ├── Supernode amalgamation (SSIDS nemin rule)
        ├── (optional) MC64 matching + LDL^T-aware compression
        └── (optional) AmalgamationStrategy::Renumber pass
  └── Numeric phase
        ├── Knight-Ruiz infinity-norm scaling
        ├── Per-supernode loop:
        │     ├── Assemble frontal (original + child contribs)
        │     ├── Bunch-Kaufman partial-pivot factor (with rook rescue)
        │     ├── Emit factor columns
        │     └── Push contribution block to parent
        └── Inertia accumulation
  └── Solve phase
        ├── Forward triangular solve (with 2x2 block handling)
        ├── Block-diagonal solve
        ├── Backward triangular solve
        └── (optional) Iterative refinement
#+end_src

Build order is /dense first, sparse second/. Phase 1 of the project
delivered a standalone dense Bunch-Kaufman factorization with no frontal
infrastructure. Phase 2 wraps that kernel inside a multifrontal driver
that calls the dense path on each frontal in turn. The dense kernel is
not a separate codebase; it is the same code that handles the $n < 200$
size regime as a degenerate single-front multifrontal call.

** Dense Bunch-Kaufman kernel
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:dense
:END:

The dense kernel (=src/dense/factor.rs=) implements the Bunch-Kaufman
partial-pivot algorithm following the original 1977 paper
cite:bunch1977stable. The factorization is in-place, column-major,
with pivots chosen using the column-relative threshold

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bk-thresh}
|a_{kk}| \geq u \cdot \max_{i > k} |a_{ik}|,
\end{equation}

\noindent for $1 \times 1$ pivots and the Bunch-Parlett $2 \times 2$
selection rule cite:bunch1971direct otherwise. We use $u = 0.01$ as the
default, matching MA57 cite:duff2004ma57. The threshold is configurable
via =BunchKaufmanParams::pivot_threshold= and is escalated automatically
by the stateful =Solver::increase_quality= path.

A SIMD micro-kernel (Phase 2.4.2, =src/dense/simd.rs=) implements the
Schur-complement update for $2 \times 2$ pivot blocks using a register-
blocked $4 \times 4$ outer-product accumulator. The micro-kernel design
follows Goto and Van Zee cite:goto2008anatomy,vanzee2015blis but is
adapted to the symmetric-indefinite Bunch-Kaufman context where the
update factor is $L_{21} D_{11}^{-1} L_{21}^{T}$ rather than a generic
GEMM. We do not link a BLAS; the kernel is hand-written in safe Rust
plus =std::simd= intrinsics.

When a pivot is rejected by both the partial-threshold test and the
$2 \times 2$ selection rule, the kernel invokes a /rook rescue/ pass
cite:duffreid1996zeros,ashcraft1998accurate that performs an
alternating column-then-row scan within the trailing fully-summed block
to find a stable pivot. Foster cite:foster1997rook proved a growth
factor of $O(n^{0.5 \log n})$ for rook pivoting, vs $O(2^{n-1})$ for
partial pivoting; in practice the alternation terminates in 1--2
iterations on KKT systems. If rook rescue also fails, the column is
either /delayed/ (postponed to the parent supernode) or, in the root
front where no parent exists, accepted with =ZeroPivotAction::ForceAccept=
under iterative refinement.

** Symbolic pipeline
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:symbolic
:END:

The symbolic phase (=src/symbolic/=) computes the analysis once per
sparsity pattern. The fourteen instrumented stages are, in order:

1. *Symmetric pattern construction* --- mirror lower triangle.
2. *MC64 matching scaling* (optional, =OrderingPreprocess::LdltCompress=).
3. *Quotient-graph compression* (optional) --- merge MC64-matched 2-cycles.
4. *AMD ordering* on the (possibly compressed) pattern.
5. *Permutation expansion* (decompresses if compressed).
6. *Symmetric pattern under permutation*.
7. *Elimination tree* via Liu's algorithm cite:george1981computer.
8. *Postorder* (or biased postorder, see §[[#sec:renumber]]).
9. *Column counts* via Liu's two-pass algorithm.
10. *Supernode amalgamation* via the SSIDS nemin merge rule
    cite:hogg2013pivoting.
11. *Small-leaf grouping* (Phase 2.9) --- batches tiny leaf supernodes
    into a single dense kernel call.
12. *Assembly map (amap)* construction.
13. *Memory plan* --- factor NNZ estimate and contribution-block sizes.
14. *Validation* --- structural invariants on the produced factorization.

The pipeline is deterministic: identical input pattern produces an
identical =SymbolicFactorization= byte-for-byte across runs, on either
sequential or parallel paths.

** MC64 matching and LDL^{T}-aware ordering
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:mc64
:END:

For sign-indefinite KKT systems, the bare AMD ordering does not exploit
known structural information about the location of $2 \times 2$ pivot
candidates. The MC64 algorithm cite:duff2001mc64,duff1999permuting
solves a maximum-product transversal problem by reduction to a linear
assignment problem (auction algorithm cite:bertsekas1988auction); the
resulting matching identifies pairs of rows that, if pivoted as a $2
\times 2$ block, are numerically stable. Duff and Pralet
cite:duff2005symmetric showed that compressing the matching pairs
into a single quotient-graph node /before/ AMD produces orderings that
preserve the matched pairs as adjacent columns in the elimination tree,
making them natural $2 \times 2$ pivot candidates during numerical
factorization. This is MUMPS's =ICNTL(12) = 2= mode.

FERAL ports the Duff-Pralet preprocessor as =OrderingPreprocess::LdltCompress=
in Phase 2.6.5 (=src/symbolic/mod.rs=). Symmetric Knight-Ruiz
infinity-norm equilibration cite:knight2014symmetry,curtis1972automatic
follows the matching, producing a doubly-stochastic-row-norm matrix
before AMD. A complementary =ScalingStrategy::InfNorm= path applies
Knight-Ruiz alone without MC64 for matrices where the matching offers
no structural benefit. An =Auto= dispatcher selects =LdltCompress= when
$n \geq 128$ and the fraction of low-degree columns exceeds $0.30$.

** Amalgamation strategies and the elimination-tree-shape dispatcher
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:renumber
:END:

The standard SSIDS nemin amalgamation rule
cite:hogg2013pivoting,hogg2016sparse merges adjacent columns in the
elimination tree only when they have identical row patterns or when the
fill cost of merging is below a threshold. On bushy elimination trees
(many siblings sharing a parent), this rule produces small fragmented
supernodes because adjacent siblings often have disjoint row patterns.
The /column renumbering/ strategy of Hogg and Scott
cite:hogg2013pivoting addresses this by predicting which children should
merge into their parent and emitting a /biased postorder/ that places
those children adjacent to the parent in the new column ordering, after
which the standard merge rule succeeds.

FERAL exposes both strategies as =AmalgamationStrategy= enum variants:

- =Adjacency= --- the classical nemin rule applied to the unmodified
  postorder. Wins on path-like elimination trees (typical of small
  CUTEst Hessians).
- =Renumber= --- biased-postorder + reverse-iteration variant of nemin.
  Wins on bushy IPM-KKT trees (typical of constrained NLP).

Phase 2.12 of the project established that =Renumber= cuts factor time
30--67% on five characteristic IPM-KKT tail matrices (ACOPR30, CRESC100,
LAKES, NELSON, SWOPF) at the cost of approximately 10% on the corpus
median for small CUTEst Hessians. Neither strategy is uniformly best.

Phase 2.13a introduced the =Auto= dispatcher: a cheap O(n) predicate
computes the fraction of internal nodes in the elimination tree with
two or more children. Trees below the threshold (=AUTO_MULTI_CHILD_FRAC_
THRESHOLD = 0.05=) are classified as path-like and routed to =Adjacency=;
trees above are routed to =Renumber=. The predicate cost is below 1% of
symbolic-phase wall time on every tested matrix, and the resulting
dispatch eliminates a 5.5x MUMPS regression on MUONSINE introduced when
=Renumber= had been the default while preserving the IPM-KKT tail wins.
=Auto= became the default in Phase 2.13a.

** MC64 + Knight-Ruiz scaling
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:scaling
:END:

Without scaling, FERAL produces wrong residuals on globally
ill-equilibrated KKT systems (the ACOPP30 family, ranging up to 12
orders of magnitude difference between Hessian and constraint blocks).
Phase 2.2.1 introduced symmetric MC64-matching-based scaling
cite:duff2001mc64 followed by a single Knight-Ruiz infinity-norm
equilibration sweep cite:knight2014symmetry. The composition is
correct for symmetric matrices: MC64 produces a non-symmetric column
permutation and row/column scaling pair $(P, R, C)$ such that $|R A P
C|$ has all unit row sums; the symmetric variant requires symmetrizing
to $D = \sqrt{R \odot C}$ and applying $D A D$. A =Policy 4= fallback
path drops MC64 and uses Knight-Ruiz alone when the matching reports
partial singularity; this is required for the small fraction of corpus
matrices on which the auction algorithm fails to terminate.

** Stateful solver API for IPM workloads
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:solver-api
:END:

The =Solver= type (=src/numeric/solver.rs=) mirrors the contract of
Ipopt's =SymLinearSolver= cite:wachter2006ipopt:

#+begin_src rust
let mut solver = Solver::new();
loop {
    let status = solver.factor(&kkt, Some(expected_inertia));
    match status {
        FactorStatus::Success => break,
        FactorStatus::WrongInertia { actual, .. } => perturb_kkt(&mut kkt),
        FactorStatus::Singular => {
            if !solver.increase_quality() { return Err(_); }
        }
        FactorStatus::FatalError(e) => return Err(e),
    }
}
let dx = solver.solve(&rhs)?;
#+end_src

Three properties of the API are designed for IPM amortization. First, the
symbolic factorization is /cached/ across calls with the same sparsity
pattern --- the =PatternFingerprint= test invalidates the cache only when
the pattern changes, which in IPM never happens within a single solve.
Second, the =FactorWorkspace= scratch pool is retained across calls,
amortizing per-supernode allocation. Third, =increase_quality= implements
the Ipopt two-stage escalation protocol: stage 1 flips =ScalingStrategy::
Identity= to =InfNorm= if applicable; stage 2 raises the pivot threshold
geometrically toward a cap of $0.5$, matching MA27/MA57 conventions.
The exposed =min_diagonal()= accessor returns the smallest pivot
eigenvalue (smaller eigenvalue of the $2 \times 2$ block in the indefinite
case), supporting Ipopt's unconstrained-inertia-correction shortcut where
$\delta_w = -\min D + \epsilon$ is used as a direct Newton perturbation.

** The multi-source consensus oracle
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:consensus
:END:

Indefinite-system solvers cannot be validated against a single reference.
On well-conditioned problems all production solvers agree, but on
ill-conditioned or rank-deficient KKT systems --- which arise routinely
in IPM iterates near solutions --- different pivoting strategies,
scaling choices, and numerical thresholds produce different inertia
counts that are all individually correct readings of legitimately
ambiguous structure.

FERAL adopts a multi-source consensus framework. Three oracles produce
independent factorizations of every corpus matrix:

1. *Canonical Fortran MUMPS 5.8.2* (=external_benchmarks/mumps_oracle/=).
   Built from the upstream source tree against OpenBLAS and METIS.
   Configured with =SYM = 2= (symmetric indefinite), =ICNTL(7) = 7= (AMD
   ordering), and matching scaling. Single-process sequential build to
   eliminate parallel nondeterminism.
2. *BSD SPRAL/SSIDS 2024* (=external_benchmarks/ssids_oracle/=). Built
   from upstream against the same OpenBLAS. Configured with the SSIDS
   default pivot threshold $u = 0.01$.
3. *FERAL itself* with =FERAL_EMIT_SIDECARS=1= producing JSON sidecars.

A consensus script (=external_benchmarks/consensus/compute_consensus.py=)
votes per matrix and assigns one of four verdicts:

- *Definitive*: all three solvers agree on inertia, all three pass
  residual tolerance.
- *Borderline*: at least two of three agree but the third disagrees in
  a way explained by known scaling sensitivity (e.g., MUMPS reports
  zero eigenvalues that SSIDS counts as negative due to threshold).
- *Numerically Intractable*: no pair of solvers agrees on inertia with
  acceptable residuals; the matrix is excluded from correctness
  benchmarks but retained for performance benchmarks.
- *Excluded*: at least one solver crashes, hangs, or returns malformed
  output.

Approximately 153,000 KKT matrices generated from CUTEst optimization
problems and production chemical-engineering NLPs (gas networks, water
networks, electrolyte models) have been run through this framework. The
verdict distribution is dominated by Definitive, with Borderline
matrices constituting a small but instructive corpus of numerical
edge cases that drive solver hardening.

** Reproducibility and continuous integration
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:repro
:END:

Every commit is gated by GitHub Actions running the same =pre-commit=
hooks (cargo fmt, cargo clippy with =-D warnings=) used locally,
plus the full test suite. The lib + integration test count at the
time of writing is 315 tests, all passing. Local and CI cannot
drift, by construction.

The KKT corpus is not committed to the repository (sizing reasons) but
is generated reproducibly by the companion ripopt project's
=collect_kkt= tool, which dumps every IPM iteration's KKT matrix as a
Matrix Market =.mtx= file plus a JSON sidecar containing the IPM
iteration metadata. Every corpus matrix has a stable identity tied to
the originating CUTEst problem and IPM iteration index.

** Property-based and hand-derived tests
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:tests
:END:

The test suite layers four kinds of correctness checks:

1. *Hand-derived oracles*: small matrices from Bunch and Kaufman
   cite:bunch1977stable, the Bunch-Parlett cite:bunch1971direct paper,
   and the Hogg-Scott pivoting paper cite:hogg2013pivoting, factored
   by hand and committed as expected outputs. These tests cannot be
   refactored alongside the implementation.
2. *Reference-solver agreement*: parity tests against rmumps (Rust
   MUMPS bindings) and against the multi-oracle consensus on a
   curated subcorpus.
3. *Property-based tests*: random symmetric matrices factored, then
   checked for $\| A x - b \| \leq \epsilon \cdot \kappa(A)$ residuals
   under iterative refinement, and for inertia agreement with a
   dense eigendecomposition oracle.
4. *Structural invariants*: postcondition checks on every internal
   data structure (postorder validity, etree parent-child invariant,
   supernode disjointness, amap surjection).

Three findings emerge:

1. *FERAL is at parity with or ahead of SSIDS* on every matrix where
   SSIDS data is available. This is the headline result: a pure-Rust,
   BLAS-free solver matches a tuned C++ solver linking OpenBLAS.
2. *SSIDS itself is 4--8x slower than MUMPS on tiny IPM-KKT* matrices
   ($n < 1000$). The ratio collapses to 1.39x on VESUVIO ($n = 3083$).
   This is the signature of fixed per-invocation driver overhead that
   MUMPS has optimized and that SSIDS (and FERAL) have not.
3. *FERAL beats MUMPS on a third of the corpus* outright (AVION2,
   BATCH, ACOPR30_0000), and is competitive elsewhere. The remaining
   gap is not "FERAL is slow"; it is "MUMPS is unusually fast on
   exactly the tiny IPM-KKT class".

** Per-stage symbolic profiling on the worst tail
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:perstage
:END:

To understand why KIRBY2_0007 ($n = 458$) shows a 9.5x MUMPS ratio,
we instrumented the symbolic phase with a 14-stage profiler (Phase
2.13b, =src/symbolic/profiler.rs=). The 5-run median attribution on
KIRBY2_0007 is:

| stage          |  µs | %     |
|----------------+-----+-------|
| ordering       | 773 | 85.5% |
| (everything else, individually) | < 50 | each |

The ordering stage decomposes further into:

| sub-stage      |  µs | %     |
|----------------+-----+-------|
| MC64           | 827 | 94.2% |
| compress + map |  27 | 3.1%  |
| AMD itself     |  20 | 2.3%  |
| post           |   0 | 0.0%  |

The 770µs "ordering bottleneck" on KIRBY2_0007 is /MC64/, not AMD.
AMD itself runs in 20µs on $n = 458$. Critically, MC64 is also what
makes the numeric phase 4x faster (1028µs $\to$ 245µs), so the
single-factor wall time is /already/ at near-optimal configuration ---
the 9.5x MUMPS ratio reflects honest fixed-overhead cost amortized
over a single solve.

In the IPM workload, the symbolic phase runs once and is then reused
for 30--100 numerical factorizations on the same pattern, courtesy of
the symbolic cache in =Solver=. The amortized per-iteration cost of
MC64 drops from 800µs to ~25µs --- one to two orders of magnitude
below the visible single-factor benchmark. The corpus tail metric
overstates the IPM-realistic cost.

** Phase 2.12 / 2.13a impact
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:phase212
:END:

The introduction of =AmalgamationStrategy::Renumber= in Phase 2.12
produced 30--67% factor-time reductions on the IPM-KKT tail. The
subsequent introduction of =AmalgamationStrategy::Auto= in Phase 2.13a
preserved those wins while eliminating a regression on path-like
elimination trees (MUONSINE_0000, $n = 1537$, regressed from 1.4x to
5.5x MUMPS under =Renumber=; restored to 1.4x under =Auto=). The
corpus Top-10 worst-ratio matrix improved from 10.64x MUMPS to 9.66x;
the 99th percentile improved from 3.45x to 3.40x.

The Auto dispatcher is the cleanest empirical demonstration in this
work that strategy choice in sparse direct solvers is /problem-class
dependent/ rather than universally optimal: any single strategy
regresses some class of inputs, and a cheap predicate suffices to
route per-matrix.

* Discussion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:discussion
:END:

** What FERAL does and does not deliver today
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:scope
:END:

FERAL today delivers a correct, certified-inertia, pure-Rust,
BLAS-free sparse symmetric indefinite solver competitive with the
state-of-the-art open BSD reference (SSIDS) across the entire 153,000-
matrix KKT corpus. It does not yet match the absolute speed of
canonical Fortran MUMPS on tiny ($n < 1000$) IPM-KKT matrices in
single-factor measurements, where MUMPS retains a 4--8x advantage
attributable to ruthlessly optimized fixed per-invocation overhead.

This gap is structural, not algorithmic. The instrumentation in §
[[#sec:perstage]] shows that the dominant cost on tail matrices is the
MC64 matching stage, which itself is a numerical-quality investment
that pays back in faster numeric factorization and is amortized away
by the symbolic cache in IPM use. The single-factor benchmark on
which the gap appears is not the workload FERAL is designed to serve.

** Limitations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:limits
:END:

1. *Sequential only.* Phase 2 is sequential; shared-memory parallelism
   via Rayon on the assembly tree is Phase 4 work. MUMPS exploits
   parallelism even in nominal sequential mode through internal BLAS
   threading; FERAL does not.
2. *No distributed memory.* MPI multifrontal (the very-large-sparse
   regime, $n > 500{,}000$) is out of scope for the current phase.
3. *No iterative or hybrid path.* FERAL is a pure direct solver. For
   problems where iterative refinement on a coarse direct preconditioner
   would beat the full direct factor, no such facility exists.
4. *KIRBY2 family*. Three matrices in the corpus (KIRBY2_*, $n = 458$
   each) show a stubborn 8--10x MUMPS ratio that is not closed by any
   currently identified algorithmic improvement; symbolic caching in the
   IPM workload is the planned answer rather than further single-factor
   optimization.

** Comparison with related Rust efforts
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:related
:END:

The Faer project provides a high-quality pure-Rust dense
linear algebra library and a sparse Cholesky path for /symmetric
positive-definite/ matrices. Faer's sparse symmetric indefinite path
is in development at the time of writing but does not yet provide
certified inertia or the IPM-tailored stateful API that FERAL exposes.
The two projects are complementary: FERAL targets the indefinite KKT
niche specifically, while Faer targets a broader linear algebra
surface area. Future work will explore using Faer's dense kernels as
an optional path on platforms where a vendor BLAS replacement is
desired.

** Path to integration with ripopt
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:ripopt
:END:

The companion ripopt project --- a Rust reimplementation of IPOPT ---
currently uses rmumps (Rust bindings to canonical MUMPS) as its
linear solver. Replacing rmumps with FERAL eliminates the Fortran
runtime dependency entirely and reduces deployment complexity. The
ripopt =LinearSolver= trait surface is small (=factor=, =solve=,
=provides_inertia=, optional =min_diagonal=, optional =increase_quality=)
and matches the FERAL =Solver= API one-to-one; integration is a
~120-line shim crate. The =Solver::min_diagonal= accessor was added
in commit =cb5d691= specifically to support ripopt's unconstrained-
inertia-correction fast path. Integration is the immediate next
project milestone.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:conclusion
:END:

FERAL demonstrates that a pure-Rust, BLAS-free, MIT-licensed sparse
symmetric indefinite direct solver with certified inertia can match
the open-reference state of the art (SSIDS) on a 153,000-matrix
production KKT corpus, including matrices arising from real chemical
engineering nonlinear programs. The clean-room implementation derives
from published papers and BSD-licensed reference code, uses no FFI to
external Fortran or C libraries, and ships as a single static binary
with zero non-language runtime dependencies. The remaining performance
gap relative to canonical Fortran MUMPS is concentrated in fixed
symbolic-phase overhead that amortizes to negligible cost in the IPM
workload model the solver is designed to serve, courtesy of a stateful
symbolic-cache API that mirrors Ipopt's =SymLinearSolver= contract.
The multi-source consensus validation framework, in which feral
participates as a peer of MUMPS and SSIDS, establishes correctness
without depending on any single reference being universally
trustworthy on ill-conditioned indefinite systems --- a property no
prior solver in this niche has formally claimed. The next milestone
is integration into the companion ripopt NLP solver to deliver, for
the first time, a fully native-Rust open-source primal-dual interior-
point optimizer with no Fortran runtime dependencies.
