Karypis and Kumar (1998) present a multilevel k-way partitioning algorithm
that directly partitions graphs into k parts rather than using recursive
bisection citep:karypis1998kway. This paper complements
[[file:./karypis1998-metis.org][the core METIS paper]] and is implemented in
METIS's =kmetis.c= driver.

** Motivation: Recursive Bisection Limitations

Recursive bisection partitions a graph into k parts by applying multilevel
bisection log_2(k) times. This has two drawbacks:

1. Each bisection optimizes a local cut without considering the global k-way
   objective. A locally optimal bisection may not lead to a globally optimal
   k-way partition.
2. The edges cut in early bisections are "frozen" — later bisections cannot
   undo poor early decisions.

Direct k-way partitioning addresses both issues by considering all k parts
simultaneously during refinement.

** The Multilevel k-way Framework

The algorithm follows the same three-phase multilevel structure as METIS
bisection:

*** Coarsening

Identical to the bisection coarsening: heavy-edge matching (HEM) or sorted
heavy-edge matching (SHEM) repeatedly contracts matched vertex pairs until
the coarsest graph has O(k) vertices. Each coarsening level halves the vertex
count.

*** Initial k-way Partition

At the coarsest level, the graph is small enough (O(100·k) vertices) to
partition directly. METIS uses recursive bisection at this stage to produce
the initial k-way partition, then switches to k-way refinement during
uncoarsening.

*** k-way Refinement

The key contribution. During uncoarsening, each projected partition is refined
using a k-way variant of FM refinement:

For each boundary vertex v in part p:
1. Compute the /gain/ of moving v to each adjacent part q:
   gain(v, p → q) = (edges to q) - (edges to p \ {v})
2. The vertex with the highest positive gain is moved
3. Moves continue greedily, with a rollback to the best state seen

The boundary restriction (only considering vertices adjacent to other parts)
makes each refinement pass O(|boundary edges|) rather than O(|E|).

** Comparison with Recursive Bisection

On standard benchmarks (METIS test suite):
- For k = 2 to 8: recursive bisection and k-way produce similar quality
- For k = 16 to 256: k-way produces 10-30% smaller edge cuts
- k-way is also faster for large k because it avoids log_2(k) bisection levels

** Relevance to FERAL

For [[file:../concepts/sparse-matrix-ordering.org][fill-reducing ordering]],
nested dissection uses /recursive bisection/ (not k-way), because the recursion
naturally produces the separator hierarchy needed for the elimination tree.
k-way partitioning is more relevant for domain decomposition and parallel load
balancing than for fill-reducing ordering.

However, understanding k-way partitioning is important for reading the METIS
codebase: the =kmetis.c= (k-way driver) and =ometis.c= (nested dissection
driver) share coarsening and refinement primitives. The k-way refinement data
structures (boundary lists, gain arrays) are also used in separator
refinement.
