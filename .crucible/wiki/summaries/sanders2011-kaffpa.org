Sanders and Schulz (2011) present KaFFPa (Karlsruhe Fast Flow Partitioner), a
multilevel graph partitioning algorithm that replaces FM refinement with
max-flow-based local improvement citep:sanders2011engineering. This is the
foundational paper for the [[file:../concepts/kahip-graph-partitioning.org][KaHIP]]
framework.

** Max-Flow-Based Local Improvement

The defining algorithmic innovation. Instead of Fiduccia-Mattheyses (FM)
refinement citep:fiduccia1982linear — which makes greedy single-vertex moves
with roll-back — KaFFPa constructs and solves a local max-flow/min-cut problem:

1. Given a partition with boundary B between parts 0 and 1, grow an /area/ A
   around B by including vertices within distance d of B
2. Construct a flow network on A: source = vertices firmly in part 0 (distance
   d from boundary), sink = vertices firmly in part 1
3. Compute the max-flow/min-cut in this network
4. The min-cut gives the optimal partition of A respecting the source/sink
   assignment, within the balance constraint
5. Apply this improved local partition to the graph

This finds the true minimum cut in the local region. FM can miss this because
it is greedy and can get trapped in local optima — it moves one vertex at a
time and relies on the roll-back heuristic to escape. Max-flow solves the local
problem exactly.

*** Complexity

Max-flow on a subgraph of size |A| costs O(|A|^2 · sqrt(|E_A|)) with
Goldberg-Tarjan push-relabel, or O(|E_A| · |V_A|^{2/3}) with
Dinic's algorithm. The area A is typically a narrow band around the
boundary, so |A| << |V|. Total refinement cost per level is comparable
to FM when A is kept small.

** V-Cycle and F-Cycle Global Search

Borrowed from multigrid methods. After one multilevel pass produces a
partition:

1. Re-coarsen the graph, but /do not contract edges on the cut/
   (this preserves the existing partition as a constraint)
2. Re-refine with flow-based improvement at each uncoarsening level

This "iterates" the multilevel framework. Each cycle can only maintain or
improve partition quality because refinement is monotone. Two strategies:

- *V-cycle:* One recursive call per level (linear in levels)
- *F-cycle:* Two recursive calls with different random seeds per level
  (exponential in levels, but truncated early; stronger quality guarantee)

METIS makes a single pass. KaHIP's cycling typically adds 2-5x to ordering
time but produces substantially better partitions.

** Three Quality Modes

| Mode   | Coarsening          | Refinement          | Cycles | Speed | Quality |
|--------+---------------------+---------------------+--------+-------+---------|
| Fast   | Random matching     | Multi-try FM        | None   | ~METIS | ~METIS  |
| Eco    | Heavy-edge matching | FM + flow (small d) | V-cycle | 2-3x  | Better  |
| Strong | Heavy-edge matching | FM + flow (large d) | F-cycle | 5-10x | Best    |

The "Fast" mode uses FM refinement only (similar to METIS). "Eco" and "Strong"
progressively add flow-based refinement and iterated cycling.

** Results

On the Walshaw benchmark and DIMACS10 challenge graphs:
- KaFFPa-Strong produces edge cuts 10-30% smaller than METIS
- KaFFPa-Eco matches or beats METIS quality in 2-3x the time
- KaFFPa-Fast is competitive with METIS in both time and quality

** Relevance to FERAL

KaFFPa's flow-based refinement is the key algorithmic innovation that
distinguishes [[file:../concepts/kahip-graph-partitioning.org][KaHIP]] from
[[file:../concepts/metis-graph-partitioning.org][METIS]]. For FERAL's KKT
matrices, the tighter partitions translate to smaller vertex separators and
less fill during nested dissection factorization.

The "Eco" mode is the sweet spot for sparse solver ordering: meaningfully
better quality than METIS with manageable overhead (2-3x).
