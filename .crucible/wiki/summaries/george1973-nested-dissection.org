Alan George (1973) introduces nested dissection as a fill-reducing ordering
strategy for sparse symmetric positive definite systems arising from regular
finite element meshes citep:george1973nested. This paper establishes the
theoretical foundation for all graph-partitioning-based orderings, including
[[file:./karypis1998-metis.org][METIS]] and
[[file:./pellegrini1996-scotch.org][SCOTCH]].

** The Nested Dissection Idea

Consider the symmetric positive definite system Ax = b where A arises from
discretization on an n x n grid (N = n^2 unknowns). The key observation: if a
set of vertices S (a /separator/) is removed from the graph G(A), the remaining
graph splits into two disconnected subgraphs G_1 and G_2. If the separator
vertices are numbered /last/, then in the reordered matrix:

- The diagonal blocks corresponding to G_1 and G_2 are independent
- Fill during factorization of each block cannot cross into the other
- Only the separator block (numbered last) couples the two halves

This is applied recursively: each half G_1, G_2 is itself bisected by a
separator, and so on until the subproblems are small enough to order by any
method (e.g., minimum degree).

** The Separator for Regular Meshes

For an n x n grid, a natural separator is a horizontal or vertical line of n
vertices that divides the grid into two (n/2) x n halves. This separator has
exactly n vertices. More generally, for a k-dimensional regular mesh, a
(k-1)-dimensional cross-section serves as the separator.

The recursion depth is O(log n), and at each level the separators collectively
contain O(n) vertices per level for 2D, giving O(n log n) total separator
vertices across all levels.

** Complexity Results

For an n x n 2D grid (N = n^2 unknowns):

| Ordering Method      | Fill (nnz in L) | Operations (flops) |
|----------------------+-----------------+--------------------|
| Natural (row-by-row) | O(N^{3/2})      | O(N^2)             |
| Band (RCM)           | O(N^{3/2})      | O(N^2)             |
| Nested dissection    | O(N log N)      | O(N^{3/2})         |

The improvement is dramatic for large meshes. For 3D grids (N = n^3), the
results are:

| Ordering Method   | Fill         | Operations   |
|-------------------+--------------+--------------|
| Natural           | O(N^{5/3})   | O(N^{7/3})   |
| Nested dissection | O(N^{4/3})   | O(N^2)       |

** The Gap: Regular vs Irregular Graphs

George's analysis depends on the existence of small, balanced separators that
are easy to find. For regular meshes, these are geometrically obvious. For
irregular graphs (unstructured meshes, constraint Jacobians, social networks),
finding small balanced separators is NP-hard in general.

This gap motivated two decades of research:
- Lipton, Rose & Tarjan (1979) citep:lipton1979generalized proved that planar
  graphs have O(sqrt(n)) separators, extending nested dissection to a broader
  class
- Karypis & Kumar (1998) citep:karypis1998fast developed the multilevel
  framework (METIS) to find good separators heuristically on arbitrary graphs
- Pellegrini & Roman (1996) citep:pellegrini1996scotch developed SCOTCH with
  composable strategy strings for flexible separator computation

** The Elimination Tree Perspective

In modern sparse solver terminology, nested dissection produces a specific
elimination tree shape: a balanced binary tree where each internal node
corresponds to a separator and each leaf corresponds to a base-case subproblem.
This balanced tree shape enables parallelism (siblings can be factored
independently) and limits the maximum frontal matrix size (bounded by the
separator size at each level).

By contrast, minimum degree orderings tend to produce "tall and skinny"
elimination trees that are harder to parallelize. This structural difference
is one reason nested dissection (METIS/SCOTCH) is preferred for parallel
sparse solvers, even when the total fill is comparable to minimum degree.

** Relevance to FERAL

Nested dissection theory establishes why METIS ordering should produce better
fill than AMD on 3D problems and irregular graphs (FERAL's KKT target
matrices). The O(N^{4/3}) vs O(N^{5/3}) fill gap for 3D problems is the
primary motivation for adding METIS ordering in Phase 3.

The balanced elimination tree structure is also relevant for FERAL's future
parallelism work (Phase 4): nested dissection orderings expose more tree-level
parallelism than minimum degree orderings.
