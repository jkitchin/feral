Duff and Reid (1983) introduced the
[[file:../concepts/multifrontal-method.org][multifrontal method]] for factoring
sparse symmetric indefinite matrices citep:duff1983multifrontal. This is the
foundational paper for the algorithmic framework that FERAL implements.

** Key Concepts

The paper introduces three central ideas. First, the frontal matrix: rather
than operating on the sparse matrix directly, the factorization works on a
sequence of dense submatrices (frontals) assembled from original entries and
child contributions. Second, the assembly tree (or elimination tree): a tree
structure that encodes dependencies between frontals, traversed in postorder.
Third, the extend-add operation: the mechanism by which a child frontal's Schur
complement (contribution block) is assembled into the parent's frontal matrix.

The combination of these ideas converts a sparse factorization problem into a
sequence of dense factorizations, allowing high-performance dense kernels to do
the computational work. The sparsity is handled entirely in the symbolic phase
(determining the tree structure, frontal sizes, and assembly maps) and the
assembly phase (scatter-gather), not in the numeric factorization itself.

** Indefinite Extension

While earlier frontal methods focused on positive definite systems, Duff and
Reid explicitly address the indefinite case. The key challenge is that pivot
failure within a frontal requires either delayed pivoting (passing the failed
variable to the parent) or relaxation of pivot criteria. The paper discusses
both approaches, though the modern treatment of delayed pivoting was refined in
later work by Hogg and Scott (2013) and the
[[file:./hogg2016-ssids.org][SSIDS implementation (2016)]].

** Relevance to FERAL

This paper is the starting point for understanding FERAL's Phase 1b sparse
architecture. The assembly tree construction, postorder traversal, extend-add
operation, and the relationship between symbolic and numeric phases all
originate here. The paper should be read before examining the SPRAL/SSIDS code,
which is FERAL's primary code inspection reference.

The [[file:../methods/two-arena-memory-model.org][two-arena memory model]] in
FERAL (FactorArena for permanent factor storage, ContribStack for transient
contribution blocks) directly reflects the memory lifetime patterns identified
in this paper: factors persist for the solve phase, while contribution blocks
are consumed by the parent and can be freed immediately.
