Duff, Hogg, and Lopez (2020) introduce a posteriori threshold pivoting (APTP),
a new pivoting strategy for sparse
[[file:../concepts/symmetric-indefinite-factorization.org][symmetric indefinite
factorization]] implemented in the SPRAL library citep:duff2020aptp. APTP
represents a significant advance over the traditional a priori threshold
pivoting used in earlier multifrontal solvers.

** The APTP Strategy

Traditional threshold pivoting (as in
[[file:./hogg2013-pivoting-strategies.org][Hogg and Scott 2013]]) checks pivot
acceptability before each elimination step. When a pivot is rejected, the
variable is delayed to the parent frontal, disrupting the dense kernel's
regularity and reducing parallelism.

APTP takes a fundamentally different approach: it performs a speculative
factorization of the entire frontal matrix assuming no pivots will be delayed
(using a standard dense kernel without pivot checks), then checks stability a
posteriori. If the factorization passes the stability check, it is accepted
as-is. If it fails, a fallback strategy handles the problematic pivots.

This "speculate then verify" approach has two major advantages. First, the
speculative factorization uses a regular dense kernel (BLAS-3, SIMD-friendly)
without the irregular control flow of pivot checking. Second, on multicore
architectures, the regular kernel parallelizes much better than the
pivot-checking variant.

** Performance Results

APTP achieves significant speedups over TPP (threshold partial pivoting) on
multicore systems, particularly for well-conditioned problems where most
speculative factorizations pass the stability check. For the difficult
indefinite problems where speculation fails frequently, the overhead of the
fallback is modest because those frontals would have had many delayed pivots
under TPP anyway.

** Relevance to FERAL

APTP is highly relevant to FERAL's Phase 2 pivot strategy design. FERAL's
Phase 1b uses =ZeroPivotAction::ForceAccept= (accept all pivots, flag for
iterative refinement), which is conceptually similar to APTP's speculative
approach but without the a posteriori stability check. When FERAL designs the
=PivotStrategy= trait in Phase 2, APTP should be considered alongside
traditional TPP and APP modes.

The APTP approach aligns well with FERAL's design philosophy of using regular
dense kernels: it keeps the [[file:../concepts/bunch-kaufman-pivoting.org][pivot
decision]] separate from the dense factorization kernel, allowing the kernel to
be optimized without pivot-checking branches in the inner loop.
